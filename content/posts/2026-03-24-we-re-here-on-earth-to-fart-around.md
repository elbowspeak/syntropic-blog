Kurt Vonnegut used to tell this story about going out to buy an envelope. His wife would say, why don't you just order them, and he'd say no, I'm going out, and he'd walk to the store, and he'd chat with the person at the counter, and maybe he'd run into someone he knew, and he'd come home with his one envelope having touched five or six lives along the way. "We're here on Earth to fart around," he said, "and don't let anyone tell you different."

I think about this all the time now, because somewhere in the last twenty years we decided that farting around was the problem. That the friction in daily life, the waiting, the showing up, the small talk, the inefficiency of having to deal with actual people to get things done, was an engineering challenge. Something to be optimized away. Sadly, we've been spectacularly successful at it; the reward for that success is a world where you can get almost anything you want without interacting to another human. And not coincidentally, we're lonely in ways that no previous generation of humans has ever had to reckon with.

### The Social Technology of Buying Weed

I keep coming back to my weed guys. In the eighties and nineties, in the Willamette Valley, Seattle, New York City, before legalization, buying weed was a whole social technology that nobody recognized as one because it looked like inefficiency. You'd call your guy, and he was always _your_ guy, because the relationship was the infrastructure, and maybe he'd be around later, or maybe tomorrow, and you'd work it out.

When you went over, you didn't just buy weed and leave. That would have been rude in a way that's hard to explain to anyone who's only ever known dispensaries. You brought something, a six-pack, some food, whatever, because this was a visit, not a transaction. You'd sit on the couch and catch up. You'd talk about the Dead or the New Jersey Giants (look it up). When the weed came out you'd pack a bowl and smoke him out, because of course you would, it was right there and you were already hanging out. Then you'd stay a little longer because you were baked and comfortable and the conversation had (seemed) to get interesting. You'd leave an hour or two later having spent an afternoon with someone you might not have otherwise ever talked to, someone whose life was different enough from yours that the overlap was genuinely enriching.

It did actually feel like ritual at the time. And I savored it. Looking back, the entire architecture of the transaction was _designed_, by culture, by necessity, by illegality, to force human connection. The friction _was_ the system, and what it produced alongside the weed transaction was community.

Now I go to a dispensary. I look at a menu on my phone. I point at a sealed package, because everything is prepackaged now, weighed and labeled somewhere offsite, and someone hands it across the counter. I pay cash because that's still how it works where I live, then I leave. The interaction lasts maybe four minutes, and it is optimized to perfection. And it feels completely dead.

Nobody knows my name. I don't know theirs. We've reduced the transaction to pure information exchange (product, payment, receipt) and stripped out everything that made the old version a genuine piece of social life. And look, I understand the arguments for legalization, I'm not nostalgic for the carceral state or pretending that prohibition wasn't a catastrophe, especially for Black and Brown communities who bore the brunt of enforcement. But something real was lost in the transition, and what was lost was the friction, and what friction generated was culture.

### Grabbing my Sleeve

I grew up going to Happy Trails record store in Corvallis, Oregon, where the guys who ran the place were stoner music lovers who introduced me to half of everything I still listen to, and we'd stand around for an hour shooting the shit about Masada and Zappa and Grant Green and PJ Harvey, and I'd walk out with something I never would have found on my own because an algorithm can't grab you by the sleeve and say _listen to this_.

I don't use Spotify or any streaming music service. I refuse to, partly because of how they treat artists but mostly because of what they represent, which is the final step in stripping the human relationship out of music entirely. Discovery used to be a social act, something that happened between people, and now it's a recommendation engine, and soon enough the music itself will be generated by AI and the whole chain from creation to consumption will run without a single human connection anywhere in it. And yes, I realize the dead record store is the most cliché version of this argument, there's more than one movie about it, but maybe the reason it keeps getting made into movies is that people recognize something true in the loss even when they can't quite articulate it without cringing at themselves (hi!).

### Weak Ties We Automated Away

This pattern repeats everywhere you look. The bodega where the owner knew your order and asked about your kids, replaced by seamless delivery apps where you rate your driver in silence. The bank teller, the travel agent, the local bookseller who'd set things aside because she knew what you liked. Every one of these was a site of what sociologists call "weak ties," those loose social connections that turn out to be socially load-bearing structures for community cohesion, and we automated them out of existence because they were slow.

The reason is always the same, and it's always persuasive in the moment: why would you spend an hour doing something you could do in five minutes? The answer is that the other fifty-five minutes were doing something important that doesn't show up in any efficiency metric, and you don't miss it until it's gone. We optimized for the explicit purpose of the transaction; get the weed, get the groceries, get the information, and we destroyed the implicit purpose, which is contact, friction, life.

### Not All Friction Is Bad

There's a version of this argument that becomes pure reactionary nostalgia, and I want to avoid that, because some friction is just suffering. Nobody misses spending three hours at the DMV. Nobody wants to go back to a world where getting a prescription meant driving to three pharmacies. The question isn't whether we should reintroduce inefficiency for its own sake, it's whether we can learn to distinguish between friction that's merely painful and friction that's low key socially load-bearing. How can we tell which friction makes your life worse and which makes it better by forcing you to fart around with other people?

The economic incentives all point one direction. Every dollar of venture capital is essentially a bet that some remaining piece of human friction can be engineered away, and the person whose friction you remove will pay you for the convenience. This is a bet that keeps paying off, which is why it keeps getting made.

Plenty of people sense that something has gone wrong, of course. States are banning social media for kids, parents are worried, there's a whole cottage industry of books about how our phones are ruining us. But the diagnosis almost always lands on content, on bullying made easier and more scalable, on algorithms feeding teenagers poison. Nobody's asking whether the deeper problem is that we've built a world where human contact is optional, where you can meet every need without the friction of dealing with another person, and whether that frictionlessness itself is what's making us sick.

### The Envelope as Ruse

Vonnegut got this: _farting around is the meaning of life._ The walk to the store was the point. The old woman you helped across the street was the point. The envelope was incidental. And yes, you can order your envelopes from Amazon and they'll arrive tomorrow in a box that was packed by someone whose bathroom breaks are timed by an algorithm, and you'll never have to leave your house, and something in you will starve without knowing what it's hungry for.

I miss my weed guys. Not because the weed was better, though the hydroponic stuff coming out of the Willamette Valley in the late eighties was gorgeous and didn't hammer you the way dispensary strains do, but because those afternoons on someone's couch were the texture of a life lived in contact with other people's lives.

These moments were "inefficient" and sometimes boring and occasionally awkward and absolutely unrepeatable now. Whether it was in an apartment in Red Hook or a farmhouse outside Eugene or a basement in Capitol Hill, the ritual was the same everywhere. But now, the conditions that created those moments of community are gone.

Legalization was the right call for all sorts of reasons, Bandcamp is great, delivery apps are incredibly valuable when we need them. But I would trade almost all of it, every sleek dispensary and FLAC download, for another afternoon farting around with my weed guy.
