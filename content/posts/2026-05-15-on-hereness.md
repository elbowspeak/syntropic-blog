For decades, the American Jewish institutional infrastructure has been operating with a mostly unquestioned normative arrangement. Jewish identity is bundled with support for the state of Israel, which is itself bundled with halakhic authority through Israeli law on personal status, marriage, conversion, burial, and the determinations of who counts as a Jew. The bundling has been so thorough and so unquestioned by the institutions that organize American Jewish life, that for many Jews it has become difficult even to conceive of a configuration in which Jewish life is something other than these three things tied together. The bundling is the air the institutions breathe. If you question it, you're a heretic; the implication being that you're not really part of the community asking.

I want to start with where this lands for me personally, because my narrow experience is part of the broader argument.

I grew up about as Orthodox as you can grow up in Corvallis, Oregon, which is to say with the practices intact but without the dense community substrate that usually makes ritual and practice feel like the natural thing to do. My parents quite rightly believed that if I was going to experience Yiddishkeit, it had to happen in the house. My parents and three other couples formally established the Corvallis Jewish Community in 1974, after the community had existed informally for a couple of decades among an even tinier population. What they were doing, without using the term, was practicing doikayt. Building Jewish life where Jews actually were, with whatever resources the place provided, in coalition with the non-Jewish neighbors among whom they lived.

My mother later ran the Seattle Jewish community school for most of a decade, so the institutional apparatus wasn't abstract to me. It was the water we swam in. My sister and I went to Zionist camps. I did all the things expected of a Gen X Jewish kid -- or as much as was possible in Corvallis, augmented with summer trips and camps back east (and one year at the Cleveland Jewish Academy -- a sex-segregated Yeshiva, while my Dad was on Sabbatical at Case Western).

Over the years, as I grew into a philosophically curious teenager, I arrived at a universalist moral framework that doesn't privilege Jews or Zionists over anyone else but that still holds Jews should have a safe place to be. During these formative years, I naively imagined Vancouver Island could be that place, far away from the fraught entanglements of "the Holy Land". This was a thought that Michael Chabon later dressed much better and more creatively in the Sitka, Alaska of _The Yiddish Policemen's Union_.

What I find now, as a somewhat formed Jewish adult who would like to live inside a community of practice, is that the available denominational options don't fit what I need in a community. Orthodoxy in its contemporary American form has aligned with the Israeli right and increasingly with the American Christian-nationalist right, which is a remarkable thing if you map the alliance structure of fifty years ago. The Reform movement holds Enlightenment values seriously but has mostly thinned the practice in ways that make the community feel insubstantial to anyone who came up with traditional liturgy. Conservative Judaism tried to thread the needle between traditional practice and modern values but kept the rabbinic authority structure mostly intact, which has produced its own contradictions and its own demographic collapse. Reconstructionist communities have done interesting work but remain small and not well-distributed geographically. The various secular Jewish formations have substance but tend to treat traditional practice as historical rather than living.

So there's no community for me to comfortably walk into, only communities adjacent to what would fit, each holding one or two of the elements I'd want and not the others. I don't think the problem isn't that I'm looking for something exotic. The problem is that the institutional architecture of American Jewish life has been organized around bundles that were deliberately constructed and aggressively propagated; via Hebrew schools, Zionist summer camps, the suppression of Ashkenazi pronunciation and cultural forms, and most visibly today through Birthright Israel. The bundles don't accommodate the position many of us actually hold, which is liturgically traditional, secular in values, anti-statist about Jewish identity, and committed to building Jewish life where we are rather than around a distant geography we don't live in.

What I'm describing is what it feels like to be orphaned. Not from Jewishness, which I proudly and lovingly practice with my family in our home, but from an institutional architecture that was built to produce a different kind of Jew than the one I've become.

The institutional-matching problem that I experience is a symptom on the surface of the structure of Jewish life in the Diaspora. The deeper issue is that communities of practice perform essential work for people who would otherwise feel socially and personally dislocated. Individually and communally, humans are prediction machines who navigate overwhelming uncertainty about almost everything that matters. It's communities of practice, (most of often religious but can also take other shapes), that provide the predictive scaffolding that makes ordinary life navigable. They reduce the cognitive and existential load of being alive by offering shared frames for handling the hardest questions: death, suffering, ethical ambiguity, the future of one's children, what to do when the world breaks. They do this through liturgy, calendar, story, embodied practice, and the daily presence of other people who are navigating the same questions in the same frame. This function is a core human need.

The question is not whether to have a community of practice but what authority structure licenses the practice. In Jewish life the available answers have been remarkably constrained. Halakhic authority dominates Israel and much of the diaspora. Institutional liberal-Protestant authority structures, translated into American Reform and Conservative Judaism, are the second option. Secular individualism, the absence of communal authority altogether, is the third. Each carries its own cost. Halakhic authority asks you to accept a frame many Jewish adults can't accept, while liberal-institutional Judaism thins the practice until the community of practice barely feel like one. And secular individualism, while seductive, loses the substance that makes communal practice work.

But there is a rarely acknowledged fourth configuration that has historical depth, contemporary carriers, and a structural answer to the institutional-matching problem that the dominant configurations don't cover. I want to spend the rest of this essay exploring it together.

## The Yiddish Bund

In 1897, two organizations were founded with competing visions for the political and cultural future of European Jewry. One was the First Zionist Congress in Basel. The other was the General Jewish Labour Bund of Lithuania, Poland, and Russia, established in Vilna. The Bund's answer to the question of Jewish political modernity was different from the Zionist answer in almost every dimension. Jewish life would be built where Jews actually lived, not relocated to a distant land. The carrier of Jewish identity would be Yiddish, not Hebrew. The political home would be socialist internationalism, with significant anarcho-syndicalist currents running in parallel through the Yiddish anarchist press and the Workmen's Circle, all of it in coalition with non-Jewish workers, not nationalist consolidation in a Jewish state. The authority structure would be community and cultural inheritance, not halakhic legislation. The orientation would be hereness, doikayt, the building of Jewish life in the places it already was

By the First World War the Bund was the largest Jewish political organization in the Russian Empire. By the 1930s it ran schools, libraries, mutual aid societies, labor unions, theater companies, publishing houses, a daily Yiddish press, and a network of cultural institutions that served hundreds of thousands of people across Eastern Europe. Alongside the Bund proper, an overlapping Yiddish anarchist movement maintained its own press, its own publishing houses, and its own institutional density, with figures like Rudolf Rocker carrying the tradition into the London and New York Yiddish-speaking working class. Warsaw in 1938 had Yiddish theater of staggering sophistication, Yiddish literature being written at a level of formal accomplishment that the language had never seen before, with Yiddish schools running curricula in mathematics and physics and history and literature. This was a genuine modern civic infrastructure, in a Jewish language, on a Jewish cultural substrate, without requiring religious authority to license what counted as legitimate Jewish life.

What happened to the Bund was not that it lost the argument, but that it was destroyed by history, through three distinct acts of suppression that together produced the illusion that Zionism had won the debate.

The first and most obvious was the Holocaust. The Bund's demographic base, the Yiddish-speaking Jewish working class of Eastern Europe, was annihilated. Zionism's demographic base, which had relocated to Palestine in the preceding decades, survived. After 1945, world Jewry contained one movement with an intact population and another whose people had been murdered. The institutional energy of the postwar period flowed accordingly. There was no debate about which position had been historically vindicated. There was only a demographic fact.

The second was the Cold War. The Bund's socialist commitments became politically unspeakable in postwar American Jewish life. The American Jewish institutional infrastructure that consolidated in the 1950s was aggressively anti-communist, partly from genuine conviction and partly from the need to demonstrate loyalty during the Red Scare. A Jewish political tradition whose core commitments included socialist internationalism and coalition with non-Jewish workers was the tradition that had to be expelled from the legitimating narrative. The Bund was erased from American Jewish history, or relegated to a footnote, or remembered as a naive position that history had refuted. What had actually happened is that the American Jewish institutional frame needed the tradition erased for its own internal political reasons.

The third vector was the Zionist state-building project itself. World Jewry's institutional energy after 1948 was channeled into the new state for reasons that were overdetermined by the Holocaust and the refugee crisis and the genuine achievement of the state's founders. That channeling came at a cost, and the cost was paid by the alternative Jewish political traditions whose institutional energy got diverted. The Bund's remaining carriers were told their position had been superseded. The Yiddish they had fought to preserve was displaced by Hebrew as the language of Jewish national life. The doikayt commitment was recoded as assimilationism or failure of Zionist imagination.

None of this refuted the Bund's underlying argument. Rather, the Bundist position was suppressed by these historical mechanisms that together produced the appearance of refutation. Understanding this distinction is vital, because suppressed positions can be reactivated when the conditions for their suppression no longer obtain.

## What survived

For me, the inspiring news is that some people in a few geographies continued the Bundist tradition beyond WWII, despite the historically contingent suppression. And there are people living it right now.

Melbourne is the most intact contemporary case of continuous Bundist cultural infrastructure, and its ongoing existence is instructive. Holocaust survivors who had been Bundists in Poland arrived in Australia in the late 1940s and found a small Jewish community in a political environment that hadn't yet been shaped by Cold War pressures the way American Jewish life had been. They built the institutions they remembered. The Kadimah Jewish Cultural Centre and National Library has been operating continuously since 1911, predating the state of Israel by nearly four decades, and it remains a living Yiddish-speaking cultural institution. SKIF, the Bund youth group, still meets. Sholem Aleichem College is a Jewish day school organized around secular Yiddish culture rather than religious observance. The Melbourne Yiddish Theater transmitted a tradition that runs back through Jacob Waislitz and Rokhl Holzer to the interwar Warsaw and Vilna theaters, and forward to the people who continued to produce new work in Melbourne, because Melbourne happened to be where they landed.

Hinde Ena Burstin is one of the voices I find most compelling in the contemporary chain. Australian-born, native Yiddish speaker, poet and translator and teacher, scholar of Yiddish women writers, lesbian (which matters because the Bundist tradition had more room for non-normative lives than the religious-Zionist synthesis that displaced it), and because the recovery of queer Yiddish writers is part of the project of reclaiming what the suppression erased. She teaches Yiddish in Melbourne and lectures internationally. Her interview series in the Yiddish Book Center's Wexler Oral History archive is one of the documents I keep returning to. She articulates the doikayt commitment in terms that contemporary rather than antiquarian. Hinde is the voice of someone doing the work now, in the language, in the place where she lives.

The Yiddish Book Center itself, based in Amherst, Massachusetts, has been doing the archival and translational work that keeps the tradition available to people who would otherwise have no access to it. The Wexler interviews are one of the most moving documents I have encountered, voices from the prewar immigrant generation and their children describing the cultural formation that produced extraordinary people across vaudeville and theater and scholarship and politics without needing the state of Israel as its organizing center. Fyvush Finkel on the Second Avenue Yiddish theater. Jacob Waislitz and Rokhl Holzer transmitting the tradition into Melbourne. Leonard Nimoy on the immigrant Boston neighborhood where my father and mother also grew up, a few years younger, in the tail end of a formation that produced Jewish life of remarkable density without any of it being routed through Zionist commitment. Listening to these voices, what becomes apparent is that the formation worked. It produced human flourishing at scale. Its disappearance from the institutional memory of contemporary American Jewry is the result of deliberate narrative curation, not any intrinsic failure of the underlying commitment.

Nimoy says somewhere in the interviews that Yiddish will die as a living language. This is partly right and partly not, and we should look at it carefully for what it implies. Yiddish is dying as the vernacular of secular Jewish life in the way it once was. The communities of native speakers outside the Haredi world are shrinking through attrition, while the speakers who remain are aging. But Yiddish is alive in the Haredi communities, where it functions as the daily vernacular of an enormous and growing population. The number of Yiddish speakers in Brooklyn or Antwerp or Jerusalem is not in decline. The language is doing fine.

What's dying, if we're honest, is not Yiddish but the secular cultural-political tradition that Yiddish carried in its earlier twentieth-century form. The Haredi world preserves the language but in a religious-authoritative frame that is structurally different from what was built by the Bund. Haredi Yiddish is the carrier of a different inheritance. The cultural-political tradition the Bund carried, with its commitments to coalition politics and Enlightenment values and the rejection of halakhic authority as the licensing structure for Jewish life, is what's at risk of disappearing if its remaining carriers can't transmit it forward. That's a different problem from the language problem, and conflating them obscures what I think is actually at stake.

The question for the rest of this essay is what it would mean to reactivate the cultural-political tradition in a contemporary form, building on the carriers who have kept it alive through the suppression, and on the people doing related work in adjacent traditions who could be invited into a broader framework.

## A federation of Bunds

I want to suggest a working term: a federation of Bunds. The phrase describes a kind of arrangement that could exist, and would draw on the historical structure of the Bund, on the continuous Bundist infrastructure in Melbourne, and on the work of related contemporary carriers in adjacent traditions.

The historical Bund was federated, not centralized. Local sections in Vilna, Warsaw, Łódź, New York, London had genuine autonomy over how the underlying cultural-political commitment was instantiated locally. They differed in their balance of religious and secular elements, in their political coloring, in their relationship to the surrounding non-Jewish working class, in their cultural emphases. What held them together wasn't institutional hierarchy or doctrinal uniformity. It was a substantive but light underlying commitment: hereness, the cultural-political-ethical inheritance carried in Yiddish, the rejection of statist concentration of Jewish identity, and the refusal of halakhic authority as the licensing structure for Jewish practice.

The contemporary version, as I'm proposing to think about it, has similar structural features. A federation of local communities of practice, varying in their specific configurations, sharing certain underlying commitments. The unifying threads are two, both structural rather than prescriptive.

The first is doikayt itself. The commitment to building Jewish life where Jews actually are, with the people who are actually there, on the cultural and ethical inheritance the community can carry locally, in conversation with the non-Jewish neighbors among whom we live. This rules out organizing Jewish life around a distant geography as the structural center. It doesn't dictate what the local life looks like.

The second is the rejection of a specific bundling: the triple-bundle that conflates Jewish identity with the state of Israel with halakhic authority through Israeli law on personal status and Jewish identification. This bundle has become the operating assumption of American Jewish institutional life over the past several decades. But it's a historically specific configuration that the Bundist tradition refused before any of its elements had taken their current form. A federation of Bunds would refuse the bundle as it currently operates. This refusal would not entail rejecting Israel as a state, or rejecting religious tradition, or rejecting rabbinic learning. It would refuse the specific institutional bundling that makes all three load-bearing for what counts as Jewish identity.

Within these two commitments, local Bunds get to vary. Some would be secular-cultural, like Melbourne. Some would carry traditional liturgy with full seriousness in a non-halakhic-authoritative frame, which is the configuration I'd want for my own community. Some would be politically socialist or anarchist in continuity with the historical Bund. Others would be more straightforwardly liberal-democratic, or anti-statist in other registers. Some would be more religiously inclined; some less. The federation accommodates the variation because the variation is the point. What makes a community part of the federation is the underlying commitment, not the local configuration.

This is the grounding that solves the institutional-matching problem. The current denominational architecture forces choices: you can have traditional liturgy with halakhic authority, or you can have Enlightenment values with thinned practice, or you can have secular identity without communal practice. The federation refuses these as the only available bundles. It says: traditional liturgy can be carried by community commitment and inherited substance, without requiring halakhic authority to license it. Enlightenment values can be carried in continuity with the cultural-political tradition that has always carried them in this strand of Jewish life, without requiring you to thin the practice to accommodate them. Communal practice can be substantial, daily, calendrical, embodied, without requiring you to accept authority structures that license it through claims you can't accept.

There are textual resources for this position within rabbinic tradition itself, though the dominant institutional frame doesn't emphasize them. The Talmud contains a strand of thought that locates Jewish identity in what you do rather than in some institutional imprimatur. There are tractates that take seriously the question of how communities of practitioners constitute meaning through their practice, without requiring that meaning to be licensed by a single authority. Most religious Jews would dispute this reading. That's fine. The argument doesn't require their assent. It requires only that the textual resources exist and can be drawn on by communities that want to ground their practice in inherited substance rather than in halakhic command.

And the historical resources are even richer. The Jewish anarchist tradition, with its century-long Yiddish press, its publishing houses, its theater, its philosophical development from Rocker through the Workmen's Circle, is one strand. The Yiddishist secular tradition is another. The Reconstructionist movement, in its earlier and more radical form, is a third. Various neo-Hasidic communities that have detached the practice from the standard institutional authority structures while keeping the substance are a fourth. The contemporary federation doesn't need to be invented from nothing. It can be assembled from existing resources, by communities that recognize themselves as carrying part of the same underlying commitment.

## What it doesn't do

The tripartite bundling of American Jewish identity has been so thorough that descriptions of alternatives tend to get read as advocacy for the alternatives themselves. So I want to preempt what might be some of the most common misreadings.

1. The federation is not an alternative to the state of Israel. Israel exists. And I believe its dissolution would be a catastrophe for many reasons, including for the well-being of Arab Israelis and Palestinians. Instead, the federation is a way of organizing diaspora Jewish life that doesn't make Israel the structural center of Jewish identity. Diaspora Jews who participate in such communities can hold whatever positions they hold on the Israeli state, Israeli politics and Israeli ethical behavior. The federation is silent on that question because it operates at a different level. It's about what Jewish life can be in the places where Jews actually live, which for half of world Jewry is somewhere other than Israel.

2. The federation is not a rejection of religious tradition. It's a refusal of a specific authority structure that has been claiming to license religious tradition. Communities within the federation can be deeply religious in their texture, can carry traditional liturgy with full seriousness, can study Talmud and observe the calendar and maintain the practices with care. What they don't do is route the authority for these practices through halakhic legislation or institutional rabbinic command. The authority comes from community commitment and inherited substance. The practice is held seriously, in a different frame.

3. The federation is not specifically Ashkenazi. The Bundist tradition was historically Ashkenazi because the demographic base was Eastern European, but the structural commitments translate into other Jewish cultural inheritances. Sephardic communities have parallel traditions of cultural autonomism and Judeo-Spanish or Judeo-Arabic carriers. Mizrahi traditions have their own non-statist political formations that were similarly suppressed by the consolidation of Israeli state-building. A contemporary federation would include local Bunds carrying these traditions on their own terms.

The federation does not currently exist at scale. The carriers are scattered, the institutions are small, the language base is thin outside the Haredi world. I'm not claiming the federation is even close to a fully realized institutional alternative. What I am claiming is that the cultural carriers exist, the historical model is available, and the structural commitments are clear. The work of recognizing existing communities as part of the same tradition, and of building new communities on the same commitments, is already underway.

To be clear, the federation is one possible configuration among several. Renewal-style communities, certain Reconstructionist communities, the Ezras Nashim and Mishkan-style egalitarian-traditional communities, various neo-Hasidic configurations: all of these are doing related work in different forms. The federation is the configuration I've been describing because it has the historical depth and structural clarity that make it useful as a worked example, but it's one viable form among others that the dominant institutional architecture has rendered almost invisible.

## Doikayt / Hereness

I want to close by evoking what the work feels like when it's being done.

It's Hinde Ena Burstin in Melbourne, teaching Yiddish and translating women poets and lecturing on the queer Yiddishist tradition, doing the work of keeping the cultural-political inheritance available to whoever will pick it up and run with it.

It's the Wexler Oral History project at the Yiddish Book Center, recording voices from the prewar immigrant generation and their children before those voices disappear, archive as active practice rather than as nostalgia. It's the Kadimah library in Melbourne, open since 1911, lending books in Yiddish to whoever walks in.

It's the Boston and Toronto and Buenos Aires neo-Hasidic and Yiddishist circles where small numbers of people are doing real practice on substrate authority rather than institutional authority.

It's my parents, in Corvallis in 1974, deciding that Yiddishkeit would happen in this place because there was nowhere else for it to happen, and getting four other families to agree, and building the institution that has held Jewish life in that town for fifty years.

None of this is at the scale that the larger federated configuration requires. But all of it is real. The question is whether the people for whom the current institutional architecture doesn't fit can recognize that the alternative exists, that it has historical depth, and that its carriers are alive and working. The work of joining the federation, or of building a local Bund where one doesn't exist, is close to our heart, is available, and doesn't require waiting for institutional permission.

The American Jewish institutional bundling has been so thorough that for many of us it's become difficult to conceive that the alternative is real. The point of this essay is to make it visible and perhaps aspirational. The federation exists already in its surviving carriers and institutions. Recognizing it as a coherent tradition that can be joined, supported, and extended is part of the work. The other part is the practice itself, which happens wherever people gather to do it, in whatever languages the local Bund can carry, on whatever inheritance it can transmit.

Doikayt. Hereness. The building of Jewish life in the place you actually are. The work has been continuous for more than a century. The inheritance is ours to claim.
