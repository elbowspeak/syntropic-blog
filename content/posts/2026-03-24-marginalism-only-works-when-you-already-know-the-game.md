The most successful economics blog is called Marginal Revolution. Tyler Cowen chose the name because of the huge diagnostic impact marginalism has had on modern economics. It begins with a way of thinking, not a set of conclusions, and the blog has spent two decades demonstrating the power of that thinking across every domain it touches.

But I think there's an assumption buried inside the marginalist framework that doesn't get enough attention: the decision-maker has to already understand the world well enough to make good trade-offs. Marginal analysis, the framework that transformed economics from moral philosophy into a working discipline, asks "what is the best move at the margin (at the edge)?" and answers that question with extraordinary precision. But it can only do this effectively after someone has already done the harder work of figuring out what the edges are, where they lead, and what the trade-offs actually mean.

This is less criticism and more acknowledging a boundary condition. It's a distinction that's too rarely acknowledge when applying economic theory to environments with high variability.

## The Margin Revolution

The **_marginal revolution_** of the 1870s gave economics its operating system. Three economists, Jevons, Menger, and Walras, working largely independently, overturned the classical idea that value comes from the labor or materials required to produce something. Value, they argued, comes from the last unit: not "is water useful?" but "how useful is one more glass of water, given how much I already have?" Alfred Marshall later unified their insights into the supply-and-demand framework that generations of economists have learned as the grammar of the discipline.

The logic is pretty simple. Decisions happen at the edge, not in the aggregate. A business doesn't ask "are workers valuable?" but "is the next worker worth hiring at this wage, given what we are already producing?" A consumer does not ask "do I like coffee?" but "is another cup of coffee worth more to me than what else I could spend that money on?" Every choice is a comparison of increments, and the logic of those incremental comparisons, once you internalize it, explains an enormous amount of how markets actually behave.

And it works as long as the decisions are made in stable, well-understood environments. In that context, marginal analysis produces genuinely powerful explanations. Commodity markets, routine pricing, established industries operating under familiar conditions: marginalism describes these systems accurately because the people within them have, through long experience, already solved a deeper problem. They know the lay of the land. They understand what their options are, how their costs behave, what their customers want. They are making fine-tuned adjustments within a world they already grasp. Marginalism is the mathematics of those fine-tuned adjustments, and for that purpose it is close to unbeatable.

The trouble begins when we ask where that understanding comes from, and what happens when it's absent.

Friedrich Hayek, one of the twentieth century's most influential economists, saw part of this clearly. In his celebrated 1945 essay "The Use of Knowledge in Society,"1 he argued that useful economic knowledge is scattered across millions of people: the factory manager who knows her equipment's quirks, the trader who senses a shift in demand, the farmer who reads his soil. No central planner could ever collect all this knowledge. But prices, Hayek argued, do something remarkable. They compress that dispersed knowledge into simple signals. When tin becomes scarcer, the price of tin rises, and everyone who uses tin economizes, without needing to know why tin is scarcer or what changed on the other side of the world. The price carries the message.

This is another deceptively simple and profound insight, and it remains one of the strongest arguments for market economies. But notice that it assumes that the people receiving the price signal know enough about their own situation to respond to it correctly. A rising price for tin only leads to efficient economizing if the manufacturer understands what tin does in her production process, what substitutes exist, how her costs will shift, and what her customers will tolerate. The price is a message, but a message is only useful to someone who speaks the language.

This is marginalism's hidden dependency. The whole framework presupposes that decision-makers have a working mental model of their world, an internal map accurate enough that when conditions change at the margin, they can read the change and respond sensibly. In familiar environments, this presupposition is so naturally satisfied that it becomes invisible. The knowledge is already baked in through years of experience, industry norms, institutional memory, trial and error. But in unfamiliar environments (new markets, post-crisis landscapes, developing economies encountering global trade for the first time) the presupposition fails. And when it fails, the elegant transmission mechanism that connects incentives to outcomes develops meaningful gaps.

There is a framework from computational neuroscience, called active inference, that offers a useful way to think about this. Developed primarily by the neuroscientist Karl Friston,2 active inference starts from the idea that all organisms, and by extension all adaptive systems, maintain internal models of their environment. These models generate predictions about what the organism will experience. When the predictions are wrong, two things can happen: the organism can update its model (learning), or it can act on the world to bring reality in line with its expectations (action). The important factor for our purposes is that the framework distinguishes sharply between two modes of behavior.

The first mode is exploitation: acting on what you already know. When your internal model is accurate, when your predictions about the world mostly come true, the smart strategy is to use that knowledge, to optimize. So you make fine-tuned adjustments at the edges. This is what marginalism describes, the behavior of someone whose map matches the territory well enough that the main task is navigating efficiently.

The second mode is exploration: actively seeking information to improve a model you recognize as inadequate.3 When your predictions are frequently wrong, when you encounter situations your mental map does not cover, the smart strategy is not to optimize within your current understanding but to invest in understanding itself. Try things. Probe the environment. Run experiments. Gather information that helps you build a better model, even if that gathering looks inefficient or irrational by the standards of someone who already knows the game.

Marginal optimization, in this framing, is what happens when the internal model is good, when the world holds few surprises. It is the exploitation phase of a deeper adaptive process. And it is genuinely the right strategy in that phase. But it is a special case, not the whole story.

Frank Knight, writing in 1921, drew a distinction that maps cleanly onto this framework. He separated measurable risk (situations where you don't know what will happen, but you know the odds, like a roulette wheel) from genuine uncertainty, where you don't even know the odds, because you've never been in this situation before and the relevant probabilities aren't just unknown but unknowable in advance.4 Knight argued that this distinction wasn't hairsplitting; entrepreneurial profit exists precisely as compensation for bearing uncertainty that nobody can calculate, price, or insure against.

If you know the odds, marginal analysis handles risk beautifully. You can compute expected values, compare them at the margin, and optimize. But genuine uncertainty, what economists call Knightian uncertainty, is exactly the situation where your internal model of the world is too impoverished to generate reliable predictions. You can't optimize at a margin you can't see.

## When incentives don't propagate

Perhaps the clearest large-scale illustration of this gap comes from development economics. The Washington Consensus, the package of market-oriented reforms promoted by the IMF and World Bank through the 1980s and 1990s, was applied marginalism at the policy level. The prescription was straightforward: remove price distortions, liberalize trade, let markets signal scarcity. Resources will flow to their best uses as people respond to corrected incentives.

The results were, to put it generously, inconsistent. Dani Rodrik observed that both supporters and critics eventually agreed the policies had not delivered the expected results.5 Growth in Latin America fell well below expectations. Success stories in Sub-Saharan Africa were sparse. William Easterly documented that while eliminating the most extreme distortions (hyperinflation, absurd black-market currency premiums) did correlate with recovery, the broader reform agenda produced wildly variable outcomes across countries.6

The most striking anomalies are the era's great development successes, China and India. They grew explosively while maintaining policies that were, by marginalist standards, exactly wrong.5 High protectionism. Limited privatization. Extensive government direction of industry. Loose fiscal discipline. The prices were "wrong," yet growth was extraordinary.

The active inference framing suggests why the marginalist prescription worked in some contexts and failed in others. Where local decision-makers already possessed good working models of the relevant economic landscape, where they understood the available production possibilities, the market structures, the institutional rules, correcting price distortions allowed the standard mechanism to operate. The signal got through because the receivers could decode it.

But where those mental models were poorly developed, where people had never operated in liberalized market regimes, where the institutional and informational infrastructure for interpreting price signals was thin, the corrected prices produced not efficient reallocation but confusion. People froze, retreated to familiar habits, or engaged in exploratory behavior that looked irrational from the marginalist perspective but was actually sensible model-building under deep uncertainty. The incentives changed, but the predicted response never materialized. The gap between the new price signal and the expected end state never closed, because the ability to read the signal was itself the missing piece.

## Creative destruction and the harder problem

Joseph Schumpeter, who gave us the concept of creative destruction in 1942,7 inadvertently identified the deepest challenge to marginalist thinking from within economics itself. The Schumpeterian entrepreneur is not someone optimizing at a known edge. She is someone hypothesizing that a different edge exists entirely, that a new combination of technologies, resources, and organizational forms will create value that the current price system does not yet reflect. She is betting that the signals are about to change because the game itself is about to change.

This is not marginal optimization. It's a bet on a different model of the world. In the language we've been developing, the entrepreneur is not exploiting but exploring at the highest possible stakes, wagering that her internal model of future economic reality is better than the market's current model.

Marginalism handles reallocation within a known landscape beautifully: the movement of labor and capital from lower-value to higher-value uses as signaled by prices. This is the process that standard economic accounts correctly celebrate, and it's genuinely important. But the harder problem, the one that drives long-run growth and structural transformation, is that the landscape itself is a conjecture. It is not given in advance but must be discovered through a process that looks much more like exploration than optimization: hypothesis formation, experimental action, model revision, and the gradual emergence of new understanding that reshapes what people believe is possible.

None of this means marginalism is wrong. It means it is a special case, the behavior of decision-makers operating in well-understood environments, where internal models are good and the dominant strategy is exploiting what you know rather than building new knowledge. That special case covers an enormous amount of daily economic activity, which is why marginalism remains indispensable. You can't run a firm, price a product, or allocate a budget without thinking at the margin.

But the moments that matter most for economic transformation, the creation of new industries, the response to structural shocks, the development trajectories of entire nations, are precisely the moments when marginalist assumptions are least likely to hold. When people don't yet understand the game they're playing, telling them to optimize at the edge is not wrong so much as a category error. The prior problem, the hard problem, is building a model of the world adequate to the territory.

George Stigler, in his 1982 Nobel lecture, noted that Hayek's account of the price system never addressed how people actually acquire the knowledge that prices then aggregate and transmit.8 That omission is the central question that determines whether economic incentives propagate into outcomes or dissipate into noise.

Marginalism tells us how people behave once they understand their world. The question it can't answer, the one that explains why the same incentives produce transformation in one place and stagnation in another, is how that understanding arises in the first place. Cowen's Marginal Revolution is a brilliant guide to the territory where the map already exists. The territory where it does not, where the map must be drawn before anyone can navigate... that is where the next revolution in economic thinking will have to begin.
