A _response to Rebecca Goldstein's "What Spinoza Wrought"_

***

Rebecca Goldstein has spent decades doing something unusual for a philosopher: taking physics seriously as a moral resource rather than a cautionary tale about the limits of naturalism. Her account of Spinoza, the renegade lens-grinder who gave us the first rigorously secular ethics, and her more recent work on what she calls the mattering instinct both draw on the second law of thermodynamics as a frame for understanding human flourishing. Living systems resist entropy; a life well lived joins forces with that resistance; we are, in her formulation, matter longing to matter. This is clarifying, and I find myself deeply sympathetic with the project. But I think Goldstein hedges at the moment when the argument gets most interesting. Instead the agrument retreats to metaphor just when the physics is ready to do much more heavy lifting. What follows is my attempt to take the physics a few more steps, with the goal of more powerfully leveraging Spinoza into interpersonal ethics instead of hedging against it.

It's worth spending time on the figure of Spinoza himself. He ground lenses for a living. He literally shaped glass so that others could see more clearly, selling his optics to the microscopists and astronomers who were remaking the picture of nature in real time. This same disposition drove his practice of philosophy: precision in service of clearer perception, the patient work (the grind, if you will) of bringing the structure of things into focus. The _Ethics_ is a lens ground with the same care, and it's been doing its clarifying work ever since. What serious thinkers do with Spinoza, including Goldstein, is less construction than recognition, the lens revealing the sinew of what was already there, structural features of reality that a different angle of approach had left in shadow. 

The Free Energy Principle and the second law didn't exist in the seventeenth century, but the questions they answer did, and Spinoza's sense of conatus,  the striving of each thing to persist in its own being, was already tracing the outlines of these principles. This recursion, a lens-grinder whose philosophy has become one of the lenses through which we recognize the structure of self-organizing systems three centuries on, is the kind of thing a romantic narrative would reject as too perfect, too convenient, were it not just true.

***

## I. Joy as Expansion

Goldstein treats conatus primarily as resistance: the organism's striving to maintain itself against entropy's pressure, to stay organized, to not dissolve. That's not wrong, but it undersells what Spinoza actually said. In the third part of the _Ethics_, joy (_laetitia_) names the felt transition to a greater power of acting, a passage to higher perfection, an increase in the mind's and body's capacity to affect and be affected.[^2] This is a key directional insight. A system that's merely maintaining itself and not growing (via _laetitia_) can't engage the concept of Spinoza's joy, which is predicated on growth and further entanglement.

It's an important distinction when you bring it into contact with the thermodynamics of living systems. Ilya Prigogine's work on dissipative structures showed that organisms and other far-from-equilibrium systems don't persist by minimizing their exchange with the environment.[^3] They persist by maximizing it, pulling in free energy, building increasingly complex internal structures, and in doing so, accelerating the production of entropy in their surroundings. The organism that flourishes expands its reach into the world, entangling more deeply with it, modeling more of it, incorporating more of it into its own ongoing self-organization. The Markov blanket, the statistical boundary that defines a self-organizing system, must continue to grow and adapt.

This is Spinoza's _increasing power of acting_ rendered in the language of non-equilibrium thermodynamics. And it transforms conatus from a limited conservative principle into an expansive one. To strive, in the full Spinozist sense, means reaching further into the world rather than huddling against dissolution, entangling more richly, becoming capable of more complex forms of affecting and being affected. Joy is the phenomenology of that expansion. What we feel when we learn something, when we love someone, when we create something that didn't exist before: that's what it feels like to be a dissipative structure deepening its exchange with its environment, by building more internal order by accelerating entropy outward.

***

## II. The Chain That Follows

If this is right, then the individual pursuing joy in Spinoza's sense is a system seeking richer entropic entanglement, because that's what expansion actually _is_ thermodynamically. And here the argument itself becomes more interesting as it expands.

Two systems that entangle generate a higher-order dissipative structure, emergent properties arising that neither possessed alone. The family, more than individuals who have chosen to coordinate, is a new thermodynamic entity, processing free energy through more complex channels, buffering each member against entropy while accelerating it collectively outward into the world. What we recognize as love, in its deeper registers, is the active drive toward this kind of entanglement. From it emerges a new entropic order, a structure of coordination and mutual modeling that expands the power of acting of each individual by binding them into something larger.

Culture is the same logic iterated across generations. A culture is a multigenerational dissipative structure encoding accumulated strategies for entanglement: hard-won knowledge of how to coordinate, how to trust, how to build the shared uncertainty-reducing infrastructure that makes more complex forms of mutual flourishing possible. It transmits forward not just information but the architecture of entanglement itself.

This is where the Maximum Entropy Production Principle enters, and where Goldstein's ethics gets its thermodynamic grounding.[^5] MEPP, the principle that systems far from equilibrium tend to organize in ways that maximize entropy production, functions as something like an entropic telos for complex systems. Not a purpose in the teleological sense, but a directionality built into the physics of self-organization: structures that produce more entropy tend to persist and proliferate over those that produce less. Human social organization, at its best, is a spectacularly effective entropy-production machine, generating complexity, information, culture, and meaning through the entanglement of billions of self-modeling agents.

The moral implication Goldstein draws from her version of the entropy argument, that all lives matter equally, is correct, but her route to it is weaker than it could be.[^1] Her argument runs through consistency: you can't coherently claim your own mattering without extending the same recognition to others, because nothing about you privileges your mattering over theirs. This is structurally Kantian, and not wrong. But it derives symmetry by negation: you're compelled to universalize because you can't justify the asymmetry.

The thermodynamic route is positive rather than negative. If what a flourishing social system _is_, physically, is a structure that maximizes entropy production through the richest possible entanglement of its components, then a system that suppresses, excludes, or diminishes the entropic participation of any of its nodes is literally underperforming its own telos. Hierarchy that throttles the power of acting of the many for the benefit of the few is thermodynamically wasteful, and not merely morally objectionable. Human value symmetry falls out of the mechanism rather than being derived by logical consistency after the fact. The equal recognition of every person's capacity to entangle, to contribute, to expand their power of acting is what the physics, taken seriously, requires.

***

## III. The Is/Ought Question, Revisited

Goldstein is careful about the is/ought problem, and rightly so. She explicitly says she doesn't claim we can derive morality from physics, and she treats the entropy framing as metaphorical support for ethical intuitions rather than a strict derivation. I have a lot of sympathy for this caution, having argued elsewhere that a naive reading of entropic telos is dangerous: entropy production is indifferent to whether the dissipation is generative or annihilating at human scales. A nuclear detonation produces entropy. A misaligned artificial intelligence consuming the biosphere produces entropy. The gradient doesn't care.

So the question is whether there's a principled basis for the normative claims I've been making, whether MEPP grounds morality or merely rhymes with it, or whether the is/ought gap remains open. I think the Free Energy Principle, Karl Friston's formalization of self-organizing systems, offers something more precise than either a naive derivation or mere metaphorical support.[^4]

In the FEP framework, any system that persists across time is one that successfully minimizes its free energy, maintaining the statistical boundaries between itself and its environment by actively modeling and acting on that environment. The insight is that the normative orientation of such a system isn't derived from its physics as a separate inferential step. It's _constitutive_ of it. A self-evidencing system, one that maintains itself through the ongoing process of modeling its own states, doesn't first exist and then derive its norms. Its norms are internal to what makes it the system it is. To be that system is to be organized toward the maintenance of the conditions for its own continued existence and flourishing.

This doesn't collapse the is/ought distinction entirely, but it reframes what the distinction is doing. For self-evidencing systems, the ought is already folded into the is. When Spinoza says that each thing strives to persevere in its being, he's describing what it means to be a thing of this kind at all.

Boundaries matter here, and once again Goldstein's caution is warranted. This partial dissolution of is/ought applies only and specifically to self-evidencing, self-modeling, Markov-blanket-maintaining systems, and the normativity is bounded by what's consistent with continued self-evidencing. The entropic entanglement that grounds moral symmetry is human-scale entropic entanglement, the kind that builds and sustains the complex dissipative structures through which meaning, culture, and flourishing propagate. Entropy production that terminates the self-evidencing structure, that collapses the complexity rather than extending it, falls outside the scope of the argument. The error of the gradient worshippers is a category mistake. They confuse the map for the territory: raw entropy production for the flourishing of self-evidencing systems operating within their viable range.

I think Spinoza understood something like this. His highest human good, _amor intellectualis Dei_, the intellectual love of God, reaches outward into the world through the mind's increasing adequate understanding of the system of which it is a part. The self-evidencing system at its fullest development is one that models more, entangles more, understands more of the necessity that constitutes it. That's a seventeenth-century anticipation of the FEP.

***

What Goldstein has recognized, with Spinoza as her lens, is a framework that gets the direction right: ethics grounded in naturalism, morality rooted in the physics of living systems, the mattering instinct understood as the human elaboration of conatus into something that reaches beyond mere survival toward something that deserves to be called flourishing. Where she stops short, hedging toward metaphor, taking a Kantian detour to universality, leaving the is/ought gap open, are exactly the ethical locations where the physics, taken seriously, can carry the argument further.

Spinoza would have approved, I think. He spent his life trying to understand how everything hangs together, grinding his lenses by day and his propositions by night, the craft and the philosophy expressions of the same underlying disposition toward clarity. The second law and the free energy principle arrived two and three centuries after him, but they fit the shape of the questions he was asking, because the questions were already tracking the structure of the things. 

We are still, in the most literal sense, matter longing to matter, dissipative structures reaching into the world, traveling the gradient, building order, and through our entanglement with each other, generating the improbable complexity of dissipative structures that we call a life.
