Companies supplying power infrastructure, liquid cooling, and high-bandwidth networking (not GPUs themselves) are exposed to demand growth without the tariff and geopolitical risk sitting on the chip layer.

**AMD as a credible second-source training platform**
ZAYA1-8B's end-to-end training on AMD Instinct is a small but meaningful proof point. Combined with AMD's position outside the H200 tariff headline risk, AMD looks increasingly attractive as an alternative supply chain for enterprises and sovereigns seeking NVIDIA independence. The market may be underweighting AMD's training TAM relative to its inference narrative.

**Intel (speculative, high-risk)**
Intel's 18A selection by Terafab is the most significant third-party validation the node has received. If 18A executes, Intel re-enters the advanced foundry market with a marquee customer at exactly the moment domestic fab incentives are peaking. The risk/reward asymmetry may be under-appreciated given Intel's depressed valuation context. *High execution risk; position sizing should reflect binary outcome profile.*

**U.S. domestic fab-adjacent suppliers**
The $640B in announced fab investment anchored by 48D creates durable demand for semiconductor equipment, specialty chemicals, and construction services regardless of which specific fabs succeed. The construction deadline cliff actually accelerates near-term equipment orders.

### Themes Becoming Less Attractive

**Western frontier API pure-plays at current margin assumptions**
The 8–25x pricing gap versus Chinese open-weight alternatives is now a structural supply chain fact, not a transient competitive moment. Valuation models for Anthropic (despite its $50B raise), OpenAI, and similar companies that assume durable API margin expansion should be stress-tested against a scenario where enterprise coding and agent workloads migrate to open-weight Chinese models within 18–24 months. The $50B Anthropic raise may reflect investor conviction on the enterprise safety/compliance moat — but that moat has a ceiling on the cost-sensitive segment.

**NVIDIA at current pricing, given tariff + competitive risk accumulation**
Three simultaneous risk vectors are building: (1) 25% tariff on H200 with a July 1 Commerce report that could escalate; (2) training diversification onto Ascend and AMD; (3) SubQ and other architectural alternatives threatening the transformer-optimized inference hardware thesis. None of these is fatal to NVIDIA's 2026–2027 numbers given the $450B capex wave, but the multiple compression risk into 2027–2028 is underpriced if even two of these vectors accelerate.

**MaaS providers at mid-volume tiers**
The GMI Cloud data shows MaaS wins decisively at ~500M tokens/month versus self-hosted, but the crossover point shifts as token volumes scale. Enterprises hitting 5B+ tokens/month on agentic workloads face a compelling ROI case for self-hosted or reserved capacity. MaaS providers serving mid-market customers whose agentic usage is scaling rapidly face customer graduation risk that is not yet visible in churn data.
