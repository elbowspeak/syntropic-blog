## Investment Implications

### Becoming More Attractive

**AMD (GPU + custom silicon)**
The 6GW OpenAI commitment is a category-defining validation. AMD now has a credible path to meaningful data center GPU market share. MI450/MI500 roadmap with 1,000x performance claim over MI300X, combined with ROCm 7.2 software maturation, addresses the historically weakest part of AMD's competitive position. *Risk: Execution on supply chain at scale; TSMC capacity allocation.*

**TSMC (advanced packaging, N3/N2)**
Every major platform — Rubin, MI450, Maia 200 — runs through TSMC. Simultaneous ramps from NVIDIA, AMD, Microsoft, and Google custom silicon create a packaging (CoWoS) and leading-edge node demand concentration that is structurally favorable. *Risk: Geopolitical Taiwan exposure remains unresolved.*

**HBM suppliers (SK Hynix, Micron)**
AMD's 6GW commitment and NVIDIA's Rubin ramp both require HBM4/HBM4E at scale. Micron's stock movement (+10% noted in early Jan 2026) reflects market recognition. HBM remains the most concentrated supply chain chokepoint in AI infrastructure. *Risk: Samsung yield improvements or DRAM market cyclicality.*

**Nuclear power operators / energy infrastructure**
Amazon's $650M nuclear data center acquisition and Microsoft's Three Mile Island deal establish nuclear-adjacent power as a premium AI infrastructure input. Utilities and independent power producers with nuclear assets in data center dense regions gain structural pricing leverage. *Risk: Regulatory, permitting, and operational complexity of nuclear restarts.*

**Inference infrastructure specialists (low-cost, high-efficiency)**
With H100 spot prices compressed and inference token costs at historical lows, the winners are operators who can run efficiently at scale — not those competing on raw GPU count. Emerging inference optimization and orchestration layer companies become acquisition targets. *Risk: Hyperscalers building equivalent capability in-house.*

### Becoming Less Attractive / Watch for Risk

**Microsoft Azure (AI differentiation)**
Two simultaneous headwinds: loss of OpenAI exclusivity removes the premier model access moat; GitHub Copilot usage-based shift may compress near-term software revenue per seat. Maia 200 is a long-term positive but doesn't address the immediate competitive repositioning required. *Market may not be fully pricing the OpenAI relationship change.*

**Pure-play GPU cloud providers (CoreWeave-class)**
Spot H100 price compression to $2.85–$3.50/hour after 64–75% declines makes the leveraged GPU-as-a-service model economically fragile. Without proprietary model access or software differentiation, these operators compete on a commodity with deteriorating margins. *Risk is underappreciated given high private market valuations in this cohort.*

**Proprietary model API businesses**
Open-weight model performance gap narrowing to 1.7% vs. closed-weight — combined with Llama 4 Scout's 10M token context window — makes the "pay for API access" model increasingly hard to justify for cost-sensitive enterprise buyers. Businesses built on proprietary model access without infrastructure or data moats face structural pressure.

**Coal-heavy grid operators**
With nuclear representing 20% of data center power and growing, and AI data centers described as 10x more capital-intensive than aluminum smelters, site selection is intensifying. Coal-dependent grids (15% of current mix) are actively disadvantaged in competing for new AI infrastructure — a long-duration structural headwind.
