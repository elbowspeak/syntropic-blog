## Causal Updates

**1. Inference commoditization is accelerating GPU spot price floors downward, but hyperscalers are attempting to hold retail pricing.**
The 1,000x per-token cost drop over three years, combined with open-source frontier models (GLM-5 at $1/$3.20 per M tokens), is structurally compressing inference API margins. AWS's retail price *increase* while spot prices remain sub-$2/hour signals a divergence: hyperscalers are absorbing spot competition by raising list prices for enterprises locked into reserved/on-demand contracts. This dynamic advantages bare-metal specialists (CoreWeave, GMI Cloud) who can offer 100% GPU utilization vs. the 10–15% overhead loss on hypervisor-based instances.

**2. Huawei Ascend's frontier-scale validation changes the effective reach of US chip export controls.**
GLM-5 training on Ascend at 744B MoE scale is a qualitative inflection point. Export controls were premised on denying access to frontier training capability. That assumption now requires revision. The policy implication: future controls may need to target model weights, algorithmic know-how, or data — not just silicon — to remain effective.

**3. Energy infrastructure is now a binding constraint on data center expansion timelines, not just capital.**
Microsoft's Brad Smith acknowledging community/regulatory friction on data center siting, combined with permitting timelines measured in years or decades for power generation, means hyperscaler CapEx commitments ($600B in 2026) will not translate 1:1 into deployed compute capacity on announced schedules. This creates a persistent supply-demand tension in GPU utilization rates even as chip supply scales.
