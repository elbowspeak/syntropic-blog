**Iran** is executing what appears to be a dual-track survival strategy. This is a rational but extremely ambitious ask. It attempts to monetize economic and geopolitical pain inflicted on the global economy without yielding the underlying capability. CSIS's framing of the conflict as "a war of endurance, not firepower" where "Iran leverages the Strait of Hormuz to raise global costs while betting it can outlast U.S. political will" appears to be exactly the playbook in execution. *(Analytical assessment — CSIS)*

This retained capability is not incidental; it is the foundation of Iran's negotiating leverage. Tehran is essentially arguing: *we are damaged but not defeated, and we can resume hostilities if terms are unacceptable.* *(Analytical assessment — CBS/CSIS force assessment)*

**The United States** is in an awkward position: it has achieved significant military degradation of Iranian capabilities but has not achieved its stated war objective of resolving the nuclear file. The key internal tension is between Trump's apparent appetite for a transactional deal (reopen Hormuz, declare victory, move on) and the administration's structural demand that nuclear constraints be part of any settlement. **Israel** is the least visible actor in current diplomatic reporting, which is itself a signal. **Hezbollah** appears to be escalating independently of — or in coordination with — Iranian diplomatic signaling, launching rockets and drones despite the extended Lebanon truce.
