## Field Trajectory

### The Convergence of Three Previously Separate Programs

The most important structural development in this field is that three historically distinct research traditions are now merging into a single coherent program:

1. **Developmental biology / morphogenesis** — How bodies build themselves
2. **Cognitive science / basal cognition** — How minds work at minimal substrates
3. **AI and computational modeling** — How to formalize and engineer intelligent systems

The TAME + RL unification paper is emblematic of this merger. The field is rapidly moving toward a position where **morphogenesis is treated as a cognitive and computational phenomenon**, not merely a genetic or biochemical one. This means the tools of AI — reinforcement learning, information theory, closed-loop control — are becoming experimental tools in developmental biology labs, not just metaphors.

### The Bioelectric Layer as a New Omics Target

The finding that transcriptomics and proteomics are structurally blind to bioelectric state is quietly significant. It implies that there is an entire "bioelectric omics" layer — spatiotemporal voltage patterns, gap junction connectivity, ion channel states — that current high-throughput biology cannot see. Expect pressure to develop new measurement modalities: voltage-sensitive fluorescent reporters at scale, spatial bioelectric mapping tools analogous to spatial transcriptomics, and computational frameworks for integrating these with existing multi-omics data. The field is at the stage genomics was in the early 1990s: the layer is known to exist and be important, but the tools for reading it systematically do not yet exist at scale.

### Living Machines Moving Toward Medical Deployment

The anthrobot → Astonishing Labs pipeline represents a credible near-term translational arc. The spontaneous neural repair behavior — not programmed, not genetic, emergent — is precisely the kind of result that attracts serious biomedical investment. The key bottleneck is characterizing the behavioral repertoire of these systems rigorously enough to satisfy regulatory frameworks that were designed for drugs and devices, not agential biological entities.

### Slime Mold as Model System Gaining Institutional Traction

Physarum is transitioning from a curiosity to a genuine model system for non-neural cognition, with Harvard Wyss now publishing on it. The mechanosensation finding is particularly important because it extends the known sensory modalities beyond chemistry — suggesting Physarum is doing something closer to physical modeling of its environment than simple gradient-following. This has direct implications for minimal robotics and distributed sensing systems.

### Cross-Pollination Vectors to Watch

- **Organizational design**: Levin's multi-scale competency framework (cognitive light cone scaling from cell to organ to organism) is a natural template for thinking about distributed decision-making in organizations and multi-agent AI systems. This conceptual export has not yet been formalized but is visible in how the ideas are being discussed in public talks.
- **Soft robotics**: Physarum mechanosensation + anthrobot self-organization together suggest a design space for robots that use morphological computation rather than explicit programming — shape-as-algorithm.
- **AI alignment**: The framework of "diverse intelligence" — in which goal-directedness and agency exist on a continuum rather than as binary properties — is a conceptually useful tool for thinking about AI systems with partial or instrumental goal structures.
