## Field Trajectory

### The convergence is becoming structural, not just rhetorical

Three years ago, connections between morphogenesis, AI, and complexity science were primarily conceptual — Levin's TAME framework, the cognitive light cone, RL-as-bioelectricity-metaphor. What the current signals show is that the convergence is now becoming *infrastructural*:

**1. Organisms as engineerable computational substrates**
The neurobot result is the clearest signal yet that the Levin Lab is deliberately engineering organisms with distinct functional subsystems (morphogenetic + neural), rather than simply observing emergent behavior in natural or minimally-modified cells. This is a turn toward *synthetic neuroscience* — designing nervous systems from first principles using living cells. The progression xenobots → anthrobots → neurobots follows a clear developmental logic: moving from shape-competent to movement-competent to signal-processing-competent synthetic organisms.

**2. Bioelectricity as the legible layer between genetics and behavior**
The zebrafish connexin paper and the evolutionary algorithm preprint both point toward the same insight: bioelectricity is the computational middle layer — above gene expression (too slow, too local) and below behavior (too complex, too integrated). The field is now generating molecular-level specificity *and* system-level formal models simultaneously, which is the configuration needed for intervention design.

**3. AI tools are becoming native to morphogenesis research**
The Organoid Profiler, AI Virtual Organoids, and ML-guided organoid culture represent a methodological shift. Previously, AI was applied *to* organoid data post-hoc. Now it is being integrated into the culture loop itself — monitoring, correcting, and predicting in real time. This creates feedback architectures that formally resemble the morphogenetic feedback loops the field is studying. There is an irony (or a deep principle) here worth watching.

**4. Basal cognition is seeking evolutionary legitimacy**
The January 2026 paper calling for basal cognition research to become "fully evolutionary" is a field-maturity signal. The community is aware that phenomenological demonstrations (maze-solving, memory in planaria) are necessary but not sufficient. The next phase requires evolutionary mechanisms — why did these capacities arise, what selection pressures maintained them, and what does that tell us about the minimal conditions for cognition? This will pull basal cognition research toward evolutionary developmental biology (evo-devo), a much larger and better-funded community.

**5. Cross-pollination into AI architecture is underway but underreported**
The TAME + RL framework paper (Seifert, Sealander, Marzen, Levin) is significant because it goes in *both directions*: it uses RL to formalize TAME, but implicitly it also suggests that biological morphogenetic systems are doing something RL-like. As AI researchers face the limits of purely statistical learning — particularly in embodied, developmental, and continual learning contexts — morphogenetic principles (target states, anatomical homeostasis, multi-scale competency) are becoming increasingly relevant. This cross-pollination is happening in preprints and workshops faster than it is appearing in journals.
