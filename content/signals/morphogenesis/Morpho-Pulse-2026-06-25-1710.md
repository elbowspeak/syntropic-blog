## Field Trajectory

The field is undergoing a **convergence of three previously distinct research cultures**: developmental biology, cognitive science, and AI/ML. What is emerging is not simply an interdisciplinary exchange but a genuine theoretical synthesis, and it's moving along three identifiable axes:

**1. From Observation to Engineering**
The field has moved decisively from describing bioelectric cognition in natural systems (planarians, *Xenopus*, slime molds) to *building* it. The neurobots paper, the minimal genetic circuits for learning paper, and the AI-driven bioelectric control preprint all reflect this shift. Engineering is now the primary validation strategy — if you can construct a system that exhibits the behavior, you understand the mechanism. This is Levin's explicit philosophy, and it's now producing results across multiple labs.

**2. The Scale Problem Is Being Foregrounded**
A recurring theme across multiple 2025–2026 papers is multi-scale competency: the idea that agency and cognition aren't properties of a single level of biological organization but emerge at every scale — cells, tissues, organs, organisms — and that these scales must be understood as nested, coupled feedback systems. The TAME framework (Technological Approach to Mind Everywhere) is the most explicit articulation of this, but the Anthrobot and neurobot work both implicitly test it. The LSC 2026 theme of "breakpoints across biological systems" maps onto the same question: where do phase transitions in cognitive complexity occur?

**3. AI Is Being Recruited as a Conceptual Tool for Biology, Not Just a Product of It**
This is perhaps the most underappreciated directional signal. The LSC 2026 framing — AI as conceptual toolbox for biological modeling — and the arXiv cognition spaces paper both reflect a reversal of the usual technology-transfer direction. Instead of biology informing AI (neuromorphic computing, evolutionary algorithms), AI's formal machinery (reinforcement learning, information theory, topological data analysis) is being applied to biological systems as an analytical lens. The Varley et al. paper identifying "brain-like complex information architectures" in embryonic *Xenopus* organoids is a direct example: it uses information-theoretic tools developed in AI to characterize biological tissue.

**Cross-pollination to watch**: The most significant cross-domain movement right now is the potential migration of **morphogenetic code concepts into organizational design and distributed AI systems**. If a group of cells with no central controller can robustly build a specific target morphology through local bioelectric signaling, the same algorithmic principles — error correction against a target state, distributed goal-directedness, multi-scale feedback — are directly applicable to decentralized AI architectures and self-organizing robotic swarms. This connection is currently being made in academic papers but has not yet crystallized into a funded research program or commercial product. That gap is closing.
