## Field Trajectory

### The Convergence Arc: Morphogenesis → Computation → Agency

The field is undergoing a transition from **descriptive to engineering mode**. The early phase of Levin-adjacent work was largely phenomenological: demonstrating that bioelectric signals matter in development, that planaria remember patterns, that frog embryos can be perturbed into novel morphologies. That phase is not complete, but a second phase is clearly layering on top of it — one defined by formalization, computation, and application.

**Three convergence lines are becoming structurally clear:**

**1. Morphogenesis as information processing, formally stated**
The Varley et al. organoid paper and the Manicka & Levin prepatterning paper together push toward a rigorous information-theoretic account of how tissues compute their own form. The vocabulary is shifting from "signals" and "gradients" to "information integration," "attractors," and "computational substrates." This is not metaphor — researchers are using the actual mathematical tools of information theory and dynamical systems. When this formalization matures, it will make bioelectric intervention design tractable in the same way that circuit theory made electronics engineering tractable.

**2. Biology informing AI, not just the reverse**
The cross-pollination is becoming bidirectional and substantive. The RL-to-basal-cognition framework (Seifert et al.), the slime mold bandit results, and the brain-like architecture in organoids all suggest that biological systems have solved problems — distributed learning, robust pattern formation, goal-directed behavior under uncertainty — that AI researchers are still struggling with. The ICDO (Institute for Computationally Designed Organisms) is structurally positioned to exploit this: it sits at the exact intersection of biological design and computational theory. Expect papers in the next 12–24 months that use biological morphogenesis principles to inspire new neural architecture designs or training paradigms.

**3. The "diverse intelligence" frame is expanding its institutional footprint**
Royal Society B special issues, new journals (*Bioelectricity*, *Collective Intelligence*), and commercial entities (Morphoceuticals, Fauna Systems) all indicate that what was a heterodox theoretical position five years ago is becoming a research program with infrastructure. The next phase of institutionalization will likely involve clinical trial design — the step where ideas either find their load-bearing constraints or collapse under them. The anthrobot wound-healing data and the hydra immortality paper both look like attempts to identify tractable clinical entry points before that reckoning arrives.

**What is notably absent** from this dataset: significant engagement from mainstream neuroscience, immunology, or clinical medicine communities. The field remains largely self-referential in its citation networks. Whether that changes — and how — will determine the pace of translation.

**2. Bioelectric state profiling will emerge as a diagnostic modality** *(Confidence: Medium)*
As the link between bioelectric patterns and pathological states (cancer, aging, developmental disorders) becomes more formalized, expect clinical interest in measuring tissue bioelectric state — analogous to how genomic sequencing became diagnostic. The Nav1.5 breast cancer paper (Djamgoz & Levin, 2024) is an early signal of this direction. Within 3–5 years, we may see bioelectric signatures proposed as cancer biomarkers in at least one disease area.

**3. The TAME framework (or successor) will be adopted outside biology** *(Confidence: Medium-Low)*
The formalization of goal-directedness and multi-scale competency in biological systems is directly relevant to AI alignment and agent design. The concepts are already appearing in philosophical AI literature. A more formal mathematical bridge — likely developed at ICDO or a cognate institution — could make TAME-derived principles actionable in AI architecture design, particularly for systems that need to operate robustly across scales or in novel environments.

**4. Morphoceuticals will face a significant translational challenge within 5 years** *(Confidence: High)*
The gap between demonstrating bioelectric control in model organisms (*Xenopus*, planaria, *Hydra*) and achieving predictable outcomes in human tissue is substantial. Ion channel pharmacology has a long history of unexpected off-target effects. The field's current excitement is scientifically justified, but the engineering complexity of targeting bioelectric states in human patients — where the "normal" bioelectric landscape is poorly characterized and highly variable — will produce setbacks. How the field responds to those setbacks will define its long-term trajectory.

**5. Xenobot/anthrobot self-replication findings will trigger regulatory and bioethics attention** *(Confidence: Medium-High)*
The self-replication capability in xenobots (noted in December 2025 media coverage) has not yet attracted substantial regulatory scrutiny based on available data, but this is unlikely to persist. As anthrobots advance toward human therapeutic use, the combination of self-replication potential and human-cell derivation will raise questions that existing regulatory frameworks are not designed to handle. Proactive engagement with regulators — not reactive — will determine whether this becomes a bottleneck or a managed transition.

**6. The "brain-like architecture in non-neural tissue" finding will seed an organoid ethics debate** *(Confidence: Medium)*
The Varley et al. finding that embryonic tissue exhibits brain-like information integration is exactly the kind of result that, if replicated, will force bioethics committees to revisit standards for organoid research. The inclusion of Jeantine Lunshof (a bioethicist) as a co-author suggests the researchers are aware of this implication and are attempting to get ahead of it. This is good scientific citizenship — and a sign the field is maturing past naive enthusiasm about its own implications.
