## Field Trajectory

Several convergences are becoming structurally important rather than incidental:

**1. The Computation-Morphogenesis Merger is Accelerating**

The Levin/Bongard collaboration — spanning Tufts, UVM, and the Institute for Computationally Designed Organisms — is systematically building the theoretical and practical infrastructure to treat morphogenesis as a programmable computational process. The prompt-to-intervention paper is a direct expression of this: if you can abstract cellular control to the level of natural language instructions mediated by AI, morphogenesis becomes an engineering discipline rather than a discovery science. This is a profound shift in the field's self-conception.

**2. Organoid Intelligence is Splitting into Two Communities**

The term "organoid intelligence" is now being used for two distinct programs: (a) organoids as biological computing substrates that might outperform silicon for certain tasks, and (b) organoids as high-fidelity models for drug testing and disease research. These communities have different funders, different ethical risk profiles, and different timelines. The AI Virtual Organoid (AIVO) framework — silicon instantiations of organoid dynamics — represents a third path that sidesteps biological complexity while inheriting some predictive benefit. Expect these communities to diverge further in 2026.

**3. Bioelectricity is Moving from Descriptive to Interventional**

The field-mediated prepatterning paper is emblematic of a broader shift: the question is no longer "what do bioelectric patterns do?" but "how do we write specific bioelectric patterns to achieve target morphologies?" This is the difference between neuroscience and brain-computer interfaces — the interventional phase unlocks entirely different categories of application and investment.

**4. The Regulatory Gap is the Critical Bottleneck**

Anthrobots are the clearest near-term clinical candidate in this entire research space, and their main advantage — no genetic modification — maps directly onto existing regulatory frameworks. But the broader field is running well ahead of any coherent regulatory category. Living biobots, organoid-chip hybrids, and bioelectric drug repurposing all sit in ambiguous regulatory spaces. The NIH's SOM Center standardization effort (noted for 2025) is the first serious institutional attempt to address this. How quickly that infrastructure develops will pace the entire commercial trajectory of the field.

**5. Anti-Aging as the Killer Application**

The Ferriss podcast framing — aging as a software problem — connects bioelectricity research to one of the best-funded areas in contemporary biology. If Levin's group can demonstrate even modest lifespan or healthspan effects through bioelectric intervention in model organisms, the funding inflow would likely be transformative. The Hydra aging paper (Kapsetaki et al., 2025 in *Bioelectricity*) is an early signal that the lab is already working in this direction. Watch this space carefully through 2026.
