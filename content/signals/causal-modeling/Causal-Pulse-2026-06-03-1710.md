## Market Position

### Where Causal AI Sits on the Adoption Curve

Causal AI is in **late early adoption**, transitioning toward **early majority** in two specific verticals (pharmaceutical commercialization, enterprise marketing analytics) while remaining in **innovator/early adopter** territory in most others.

The clearest adoption curve marker is **methodology standardization**: when practitioners stop debating whether to use causal inference and start debating *which implementation* to use, the methodology has crossed the chasm. Marketing Mix Modeling is at that inflection point now — the DeepCausalMMM and PyMC-Marketing releases, combined with enterprise vendor positioning from Measured and Recast, indicate the debate has shifted from "should we use causal MMM?" to "which causal MMM framework fits our stack?" Clinical AI is approximately 12–18 months behind MMM on this trajectory.

**What is shifting, specifically:**

1. **The deployment unit is changing.** Causal AI is moving from standalone models (a causal discovery algorithm, a treatment effect estimator) toward embedded components within larger enterprise systems — causaLens's "Digital Workers" framing is the clearest commercial articulation of this. The causal reasoning layer becomes infrastructure, not product.

2. **Open source is pulling enterprise demand, not just research demand.** DoWhy at 1M+ installations, active AWS collaboration, and PyMC-Marketing releasing a production MMM toolkit all indicate the open-source layer is mature enough that enterprises are building on it rather than waiting for commercial vendors. This compresses the timeline for commercial adoption but also commoditizes the base layer, forcing vendors to compete on integration, support, and domain-specific models rather than core algorithms.

3. **The LLM integration question is now unavoidable.** The causaLens/Gemini partnership forces the question every causal AI vendor must now answer: where does the causal reasoning layer sit relative to the LLM layer? The architecturally correct answer — causal models ground and constrain LLM outputs — is becoming the commercial answer as well. Vendors who frame causal AI as *competing* with LLMs are losing the positioning battle; vendors who frame it as *completing* LLMs are winning enterprise conversations.

4. **Healthcare regulatory pressure is a forcing function.** The JAMIA framing of clinical AI as a causal intervention requiring causal validation methodology is not purely academic — it anticipates regulatory requirements. FDA guidance on AI/ML-based software as a medical device is moving in this direction. Vendors who build causal validation into their clinical AI pipelines now are building regulatory moats, not just methodological ones.
