Any seed or Series A company attacking this gap with defensible causal methodology deserves a close look. **4. Standalone causal AI platforms without vertical specificity — LOWER conviction**
The Causal.app acquisition outcome is instructive. A horizontally-positioned "causal AI" product without domain-specific IP or a locked-in vertical use case faces compression from both directions: domain-specific causal AI tools above (Causaly in pharma) and general-purpose LLM agents below (OpenAI, Anthropic). The middle is a difficult place to survive independently.

**5. Consumer causal health at pre-seed — HIGH RISK**

The clinical validation bar is real, the regulatory environment for health claims is tightening, and consumer health subscription churn is brutal. **The talent pipeline risk from OpenAI's causal inference research recruitment is underappreciated.** Applied causal inference researchers (econometricians, causal ML specialists) are a genuinely scarce resource. OpenAI recruiting from this pool — even for research roles — creates wage pressure and availability constraints for causal AI startups that depend on this talent for their core IP. Companies that have already assembled causal inference teams are relatively protected; those still hiring face a more competitive market.

**The "causal AI" label is becoming a marketing claim rather than a technical standard.** As the term proliferates, the risk of investor and buyer confusion increases. The commercially meaningful distinction — causal *inference* (identification of mechanisms, counterfactual reasoning, do-calculus) versus causal *ML* (using causal structure to improve prediction robustness) — is not consistently applied in vendor marketing. Due diligence on causal AI investments should require a clear technical answer to: "What is your identification strategy?" and "What is your causal graph estimation method?" Vendors that cannot answer these questions in technically precise terms are applying the label loosely.
