## Infrastructure Assessment

U.S. grid-scale storage is in an execution phase, not a planning phase. The 24.3 GW target for 2026 is backed by named projects — Lunis Creek (621 MW, Texas), Clear Fork Creek (600 MW, Texas), SMUD's 160 MW / 640 MWh project — not conceptual pipelines. The Texas market continues to dominate in project scale, driven by ERCOT's energy-only market structure that rewards fast-responding dispatchable resources without requiring capacity market participation.

**Deployment pace**: Accelerating. The 62% YoY increase in planned additions represents genuine hardware going in the ground, supported by $25.2B in capex. The BTM market (14.8 GW) is growing faster proportionally than utility-scale, which reflects both commercial/industrial demand response growth and the VPP policy push at the state level.

**Primary bottleneck — transmission**: The DOE's cancellation of Grain Belt Express loan guarantees and JTIQ grants is the most significant infrastructure risk this week. Storage projects can be sited and permitted faster than transmission lines, but without grid connectivity, oversized storage deployments in generation-rich corridors create curtailment exposure. Europe faces an analogous problem: nearly 40% of transmission lines are over 40 years old, and annual grid investment is still trailing renewable and storage deployment rates.

**Secondary bottleneck — critical minerals**: The simultaneous tightening of lithium (China mine suspensions + VAT policy), nickel (Indonesia quota cuts), and cobalt/black mass (DRC export tensions, India export halt) supply chains creates a cost floor risk for 2026–2027 project procurement. Developers locking in equipment contracts now face spot price uncertainty; those with long-term supply agreements from 2024 or early 2025 are better positioned.

**Momentum shifts**: LDES is the notable momentum shift this week. Form Energy's Google contract is a category-defining event that expands the addressable market for multi-day iron-air storage from regulated utilities alone to the entire commercial and industrial sector. If hyperscale data centers become a second demand channel for LDES alongside utilities, the technology's path to cost reduction accelerates through volume, and the 18.5 GW by 2030 LDES projection likely proves conservative.
