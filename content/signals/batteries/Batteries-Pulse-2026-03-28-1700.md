## Investment Implications

### Themes Becoming More Attractive

**Domestic battery materials and FEOC-compliant supply chains**
The convergence of 82–132% potential tariff exposure on imported lithium-ion batteries, FEOC compliance timelines, and active U.S. government bilateral agreements (Argentina, Japan) creates a structural premium for domestic or allied-nation battery materials. Albemarle is the most direct beneficiary — its North Carolina lithium project now has Japanese equity participation and government-level strategic backing. Livent, Piedmont Lithium, and other domestic lithium developers carry similar strategic tailwinds. The risk is execution: permitting and ramp timelines remain long relative to the urgency of demand.

**Long-duration energy storage pure plays**
Form Energy's coal-replacement contracting with multiple investment-grade utilities represents a commercial inflection that the market has not fully priced into the sector. The challenge is that most serious LDES pure plays (Form Energy, Highview Power, Energy Vault) are not yet public, limiting direct equity exposure. For public market investors, utilities with the largest LDES procurement commitments (Xcel Energy, Southern Company) carry embedded LDES exposure. ESS Tech (publicly listed) is explicitly in a "survival battle" per its own characterization — high risk, but a potential acquisition target if its iron flow technology proves cost-competitive.

**Sodium-ion supply chain and adjacent materials**
CATL's sodium-ion mass production launch in 2026 creates demand pull for manganese, iron, and hard carbon (sodium-ion anode material) — none of which face the geopolitical supply constraints of lithium and cobalt. Companies positioned in hard carbon precursor materials or sodium-based cathode manufacturing are early-stage but strategically positioned for a chemistry shift that the world's largest battery maker is now publicly committed to.

**Grid-scale storage project developers with contracted pipelines and U.S. domestic content**
The 18 GW annual U.S. deployment pace sustains strong revenue visibility for project developers and integrators with contracted pipelines insulated from tariff exposure. Copenhagen Infrastructure Partners' acquisition of the Beehive BESS (250 MW/1 GWh, Arizona) exemplifies infrastructure capital rotating into storage as a yield-generating asset class. Developers with signed PPAs, domestic content qualifications for IRA adders, and construction-ready projects trade at a premium to those still in development.

### Themes Becoming Less Attractive

**Lithium-ion BESS projects dependent on Chinese cell imports**
The tariff and FEOC exposure is not a tail risk — it is current policy creating current cost increases. Projects underwritten on 2024 cost curves that have not yet reached final investment decision are carrying unmodeled cost exposure. This is particularly acute for mid-market developers without the procurement scale to negotiate alternative supply or absorb margin compression.

**Early-stage solid-state battery equity plays targeting automotive volume**
The 2026 signals confirm solid-state is real and advancing — but the automotive volume timeline has not accelerated materially. Defense and specialty applications (motorcycles, drones) are the near-term market. Investors who priced solid-state companies on 2025–2027 automotive volume assumptions should revisit those models. The exception is companies that have explicitly pivoted to defense (Sion Power) or specialty OEM applications where near-term revenue is achievable.

**Lithium miners exposed to sustained oversupply**
The Fastmarkets projection of a 1,500-tonne deficit in 2026 (versus 10,000-tonne oversupply in 2025) suggests market rebalancing is underway, and the 125% price surge cited over the prior year reflects that expectation being priced in. However, CATL's sodium-ion mass production launch introduces a medium-term demand substitution risk for lithium in the 150–200 Wh/kg application range. Lithium miners with high cost curves or single-chemistry customer concentration carry asymmetric downside if sodium-ion adoption accelerates faster than current consensus.

The market appears to be underpricing the **FEOC compliance cost shock** for projects currently in late-stage development. The 56–69% cost increase already realized is widely understood; the additional 82–132% combined tariff scenario from Morgan Lewis analysis is less consistently reflected in project developer valuations and utility procurement budgets. Any utility or developer with material 2026–2027 procurement exposure that has not publicly addressed FEOC compliance strategy deserves scrutiny.

Conversely, the **LDES policy gap** identified by C2ES — LDES not fitting resource adequacy frameworks — may be creating a valuation discount on LDES technologies that is larger than warranted given the contracting activity already underway with investment-grade utilities. The market is waiting for federal regulatory clarity that may arrive on a faster timeline than consensus expects, given the coal retirement urgency in MISO and SPP.
