## Infrastructure Assessment

Grid-scale energy storage has entered a phase of genuine industrial deployment rather than demonstration-scale buildout. The February 2026 monthly figures (6.9 GW / 17.1 GWh globally) and the U.S. annual pipeline (24 GW planned) represent a step-change in the pace of physical infrastructure construction.

**Momentum is real, but geographically uneven.** China and Australia are operationalizing capacity faster than North America and Europe. Australia's single 2.4 GWh project being the largest globally this month is notable — it suggests large-format projects are now clearing permitting and financing at scale outside the U.S. The U.S. is building an enormous announced pipeline (approaching 300 GWh in Q1 2026 alone) but the conversion rate from announcement to commissioning remains the critical unknown.

**Chemistry diversification is accelerating faster than anticipated.** Sodium-ion is simultaneously entering grid pilots and mass-produced EVs in the same week. Iron-air is moving from factory testing to state-contracted field deployment. Semi-solid-state is in production vehicles. This is not a single-chemistry market anymore — the era of lithium-ion monoculture in new deployments is ending at the margin.

**The primary bottlenecks are:**
1. **Interconnection and permitting** — the gap between announced and commissioned U.S. capacity reflects queue delays, not demand weakness
2. **Supply chain localization lag** — domestic processing capacity being funded now won't relieve 2026 procurement constraints
3. **Trade policy uncertainty** — FEOC rules and tariffs on Chinese components create near-term cost headwinds for U.S. developers sourcing conventional LFP systems
4. **LDES commercial validation** — iron-air and iron-sodium are entering field testing, not yet at scale. Multi-day storage remains a critical gap for full grid decarbonization

- **Trade policy cost pass-through**: Utility-scale storage developers with signed PPAs before 2026 tariff escalation may face margin compression if they cannot pass through increased component costs. This is an earnings risk for developers, not just a supply chain abstraction.
- **Interconnection queue as a value destroyer**: The gap between the 24 GW U.S. pipeline and realistic commissioning creates risk for any investment thesis premised on specific project timelines. Developers with projects already in late-stage queue have a durable competitive advantage that is underappreciated.
- **Solid-state battery hype cycle**: The volume of solid-state announcements — particularly from Chinese manufacturers — warrants skepticism about production yield rates and actual energy density in shipped products versus press release specifications. Chery's claimed 600 Wh/kg is extraordinary if real; that number should be treated as unverified until independently validated. Investors in solid-state-dependent EV platforms should be cautious about pricing in near-term solid-state advantages.
- **Lithium price recovery + data center demand**: If the 80%+ data center storage demand projection materializes alongside continued grid storage growth, lithium markets could tighten faster than supply-side investments (which have long lead times) can respond. This is a potentially underpriced upside risk for lithium producers who survived the 2023–2025 downcycle with balance sheets intact.
