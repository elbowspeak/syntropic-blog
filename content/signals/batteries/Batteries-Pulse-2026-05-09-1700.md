**Where bottlenecks are tightening**: Three simultaneous constraints are compressing the build rate below what the demand signal would otherwise support. First, the lithium carbonate price spike (78.3% in one month) is disrupting project-level economics for contracts written against prior price assumptions. Second, FEOC compliance is a live procurement constraint in 2026, not a future planning assumption — developers cannot freely source the lowest-cost components if they require IRA tax credits. Third, transmission interconnection queues remain the binding constraint for paired solar-plus-storage projects in most markets, with the DOE's SPARK reconductoring program and FERC's large-load process both representing policy responses that will take 12–24 months to produce grid capacity.

**Notable gaps**: Northvolt's bankruptcy creates a Western manufacturing hole that Lyten's acquisition interest partially addresses but does not fill at scale. The vanadium flow and zinc hybrid cathode systems targeting 10-hour dispatch in California's Viejas community remain meaningful but small relative to the grid's LDES needs. The gap between announced LDES projects and commissioned capacity remains wide — most LDES technologies that matter (iron-air, iron-sodium, vanadium flow at scale) are in early commercial or late pilot phase, not volume deployment.

**Net assessment**: The storage industry is building faster than any prior period in its history, against a demand backdrop (AI data centers, coal retirement, EV charging) that is itself accelerating. The risk is not insufficient demand — it is whether supply chains, transmission infrastructure, and policy frameworks can scale commensurately. The FEOC constraint and Chinese mineral pricing leverage represent the two variables most likely to cause deployment shortfalls against the 24 GW U.S. target.
