## Infrastructure Assessment

Energy storage infrastructure is in a genuine deployment acceleration phase, not a paper-announcement phase. The US Q1 2026 record, EU's 10× cumulative growth since 2021, and the doubling of global grid-scale deployments in 2026 are all concrete installed-capacity datapoints — not projections. LFP remains the workhorse chemistry with effectively no near-term challenge to its utility-scale dominance, but sodium-ion is now a 12–18 month story rather than a 5-year story, with CATL's September 2026 grid storage rollout as the near-term inflection.

The most significant structural bottleneck has shifted from technology maturity to transmission infrastructure. Storage can be deployed in 18–36 months; transmission takes 12–17 years. This mismatch is now large enough to affect project economics materially, particularly in congested regions like the US Northeast, California, and Central Europe.

Long-duration storage is moving from demonstration to early commercial deployment. Form Energy's year of continuous grid operation and Hydrostor's California approval represent genuine capability milestones, but LDES remains only 6% of total installations. The gap between LDES's technical readiness and its market share reflects project finance conservatism and offtake structure complexity — both of which typically resolve 3–5 years after the first reliable operational data emerges.

The US-China cost gap on turnkey systems ($230–320/kWh vs. $90–130/kWh) is the defining commercial tension in the global market. Domestic gigafactories (Panasonic Kansas, CATL Hungary, CATL-Stellantis Zaragoza) are the mechanism for closing it, but full capacity ramp for most announced facilities is 2026–2028, meaning the gap persists through at least 2027.
