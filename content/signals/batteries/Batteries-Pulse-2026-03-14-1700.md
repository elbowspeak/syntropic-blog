## Infrastructure Assessment

US grid-scale battery storage is in an unmistakable deployment surge. The 37.4 GW of operational capacity as of October 2025 — combined with 19 GW under construction and a 187 GW pipeline to 2030 — represents a market that has genuinely crossed from emerging to mainstream infrastructure. The 2025 buildout record being broken in 2026 (forecasted at 24.3 GW) confirms that storage is now the default capacity resource for utilities managing renewable integration and peak demand.

**However, three friction points deserve serious attention this week:**

1. **The FEOC constraint is real and tightening.** With Chinese suppliers effectively restricted from US projects commencing in 2026 and beyond, the supply base for battery cells and systems narrows considerably. Panasonic's Kansas factory, Samsung SDI and SK On's planned LFP production ramp, and domestic cathode investments (e.g., Toyota Tsusho's South Korean stake) are responses to this gap — but they are not yet sufficient to replace Chinese capacity at current deployment pace.

2. **The contraction signal contradicts the headline numbers.** The 11% utility-scale contraction forecast for 2026 (from supply chain constraints) sitting alongside the EIA's 24.3 GW deployment forecast is not a contradiction to dismiss. The EIA number reflects projects already under construction or permitted; the contraction refers to new project starts and order flow. The pipeline entering 2027 and 2028 may be thinner than the current headlines suggest.

3. **Long-duration storage is progressing but not yet at grid scale.** Form Energy, Highview Power, and Invinity are deploying real hardware, and California's $247M program is pushing non-lithium technologies forward. But 2.4 GW of total installed LDES globally is a fraction of what's needed for deep decarbonization. The gap between current deployment and the 18.5 GW 2030 forecast requires significant acceleration that is not yet visible in commissioning data.

The transmission and grid modernization picture is structurally problematic in the medium term. With 40% of European transmission lines over 40 years old and much of the US grid in a similar condition, the integration bottleneck is not just battery supply — it is wires and substations. HVDC market growth (projected at 10.9% CAGR to $67.6B by 2036) reflects this investment imperative, but permitting and construction timelines mean the grid will remain a constraint on storage utilization for years.

- Form Energy's iron-air technology, if it scales as projected, will be the most disruptive LDES entrant in the 2027–2029 window given its 100-hour duration capability and earth-abundant materials.

## Investment Implications

### Themes Becoming More Attractive

**Non-Chinese battery cell and system manufacturers (near-term scarcity premium)**
The FEOC wall is creating a structural supply shortage for US-compliant battery systems. Panasonic Energy (Kansas), LG Energy Solution, Samsung SDI, and SK On are direct beneficiaries — their US-based or FEOC-compliant production commands pricing power that didn't exist 18 months ago. The market may be underpricing this supplier leverage, particularly for Panasonic given its 32 GWh Kansas facility is operational today.

**Domestic lithium processing and DLE**
The midstream bottleneck is the most underleveraged investment theme in the battery supply chain right now. Standard Lithium, Lilac Solutions, and the Smackover/Trafigura project all sit at the intersection of DLE technology and US domestic processing capacity — the exact gap Wood Mackenzie identifies as most critical. Thacker Pass (Lithium Americas) at $1.3–1.6B Phase 1 capex is the largest single US upstream bet, with a credible 2026 construction timeline.

**Long-duration storage developers with commercial deployments**
Form Energy (iron-air, 100-hour duration), Invinity (flow batteries), and Highview Power (liquid air) are all past the demonstration stage and into commercial deployments. California's $247M LDES program and similar state-level programs provide non-dilutive capital that de-risks early commercial projects. These remain early-stage bets, but the investment gap relative to lithium-ion BESS is narrowing as grid operators increasingly value duration over raw capacity.

**HVDC and grid infrastructure**
The HVDC market's 10.9% CAGR to $67.6B by 2036 is driven by aging grid infrastructure and offshore wind integration — two dynamics that are not going away regardless of near-term policy shifts. Siemens Energy, ABB, and GE Vernova are the primary beneficiaries of this buildout. This theme is less exposed to FEOC risk and battery chemistry uncertainty than pure storage plays.

### Themes With Elevated Risk

**US utility-scale BESS developers dependent on new project starts in 2026–2027**
The contradiction between the EIA deployment forecast and the supply chain contraction warning is a real risk for developers whose business models depend on new contract flow rather than existing construction backlogs. Companies without a locked-in pipeline may face margin compression and project delays simultaneously.

**Solid-state battery pure-plays (beyond QuantumScape)**
Toyota and Samsung SDI's 2027 mass production targets are likely to slip, and the capital intensity of solid-state manufacturing is becoming clearer. QuantumScape's Eagle Line is genuinely differentiated, but its path to automotive volume production remains multi-year. Investors pricing in solid-state at scale before 2028 are likely ahead of the technology.

**VPP software and aggregation platforms**
The 2/4 maturity score is a warning sign for companies selling VPP capability as equivalent to dispatchable generation. Until grid operators formally accept VPP resources as capacity resources in all major markets, the monetization ceiling remains uncertain. 2026 may be clarifying — but the risk is a prolonged period of regulatory ambiguity that caps addressable market size.

### Key Valuation Disconnect to Watch
The market is currently pricing the US battery storage deployment boom as linear and uninterrupted. The combination of FEOC supply constraints, midstream lithium processing bottlenecks, and a supply chain contraction forecast suggests the 2027–2028 installation curve will be bumpier than current consensus. Developers and equipment suppliers with 2027–2028 revenue dependencies based on today's pipeline assumptions may be exposed to a re-rating if new project starts slow materially in H2 2026.
