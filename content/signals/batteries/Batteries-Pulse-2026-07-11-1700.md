## Infrastructure Assessment

The U.S. and global storage buildout is unambiguously accelerating, not plateauing: 24 GW of planned 2026 U.S. utility-scale additions (up 60% over 2025's record) and a global jump toward 600 GWh of BESS shipments confirm storage has become the fastest-growing segment of the energy transition, now pulled as much by AI data-center load as by renewables integration. Texas remains the proof case that merchant-market economics plus fast interconnection outbuilds regulated markets.

The bottleneck has shifted from cell supply to transmission and interconnection. HVDC megaprojects (Kansas-Missouri-Illinois-Indiana, Virginia's underground 525kV line, China's Gansu-Zhejiang ±800kV VSC line) are finally breaking ground after years of planning, but timelines (2029, 2027-2032) show transmission remains the multi-year critical path, not batteries. DOE's Section 403 intervention on data-center interconnection is a signal that federal regulators view this as urgent enough to override normal FERC process timelines — a tacit admission the queue system is failing to keep pace with load growth.

On chemistry, the sodium-ion transition from pilot to gigafactory scale (CATL's 60 GWh order, BYD's ~50 GWh capacity) is the most concrete "new chemistry actually shipping" story of the year, while solid-state remains in the pilot-line-to-scale-up gap (QuantumScape's Eagle Line is real but small). Lithium price recovery (+58% YTD) complicates the cost-down narrative for incumbent LFP/NMC just as sodium-ion arrives to compete on cost — a genuinely uncertain pricing dynamic worth watching next quarter.

Manufacturing consolidation is real: Northvolt's collapse and asset absorption by Lyten confirms that Western gigafactory bets without bankable offtake are being weeded out, while China/CATL continue to consolidate share through vertical integration and now chemistry diversification.
