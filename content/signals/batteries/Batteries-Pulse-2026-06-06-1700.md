## Infrastructure Assessment

The energy storage infrastructure buildout is operating in two distinct registers simultaneously: **deployment is accelerating faster than most 2024 forecasts anticipated, while supply chain stress is building underneath the surface.**

On the deployment side, the numbers are unambiguous. Global grid-scale storage additions in the first nine months of 2025 reached 49.4 GW/136.5 GWh — a 36% year-over-year increase. February 2026 alone saw 6.9 GW/17.1 GWh commissioned globally, a 60% jump from February 2025. The U.S. pipeline for 2026 at 24 GW is not speculative — it reflects projects already in late-stage development or early construction. LFP chemistry dominance is complete, pricing has stabilized at competitive levels, and project finance structures are mature enough to support rapid replication. The Texas/California/Arizona concentration means that three state regulatory environments are effectively setting the pace for national U.S. storage deployment.

The **primary deployment bottleneck is not technology or financing — it is interconnection queue backlog**, now visibly aggravated by hyperscale data center load additions entering the queue at a pace that grid operators were not dimensioned to handle. The DTE/hyperscale storage contract is a market-driven workaround, but it is a workaround nonetheless. Until interconnection reform materializes at scale, queue congestion will constrain the rate at which announced projects become operational assets.

The **supply chain story is more concerning than the deployment story suggests**. Lithium's 95% price spike in two months, concurrent with Zimbabwe's export suspension and China's persistent processing dominance, indicates the multi-year glut that suppressed prices through 2024–2025 has definitively ended. If CATL's sodium-ion rollout proceeds as announced, it could provide meaningful medium-term relief — but Q4 2026 production start to meaningful market displacement is a 2028–2030 story at earliest. In the intervening period, developers committing to large LFP projects at 2025 cost assumptions face real exposure to input cost escalation.

**Momentum is clearly positive** — market sizing, deployment rates, manufacturing investment, and policy tailwinds are all aligned. The risk is that the speed of the buildout is now outpacing the supply chain's ability to deliver at the prices that made the economics work.

**Sodium-ion adjacent plays** deserve elevated attention following CATL's manufacturing confirmation. Companies positioned in sodium-ion materials (sodium precursors, hard carbon anodes), as well as Tiamat in Europe as the leading Western sodium-ion pure-play, are worth monitoring. The risk is execution timeline; the opportunity is being early to a chemistry that could displace 30–40% of lithium demand within a decade.

**European battery manufacturing infrastructure** — both the CATL/Stellantis Zaragoza JV and Hithium's Navarre facility — represents a bet on European energy sovereignty that is increasingly backed by formal capital commitments rather than letters of intent. For investors with European industrial exposure, these facilities create domestic supply chain anchors that reduce import dependency risk.

**QuantumScape (QS)** is at an inflection point where pilot line execution and field testing results will drive significant valuation moves in either direction. The PowerCo milestone financing structure and multi-OEM partnership additions reduce the binary risk that characterized the company 18 months ago. For investors with high risk tolerance and long time horizons, the current period — between pilot line inauguration and first field test results — may represent the last entry point before data either validates or invalidates the technology at scale.

### Themes Becoming Less Attractive / Risks to Monitor

**Developers with unhedged lithium exposure in 2026–2027 project pipelines** face a material cost risk that may not be reflected in project IRRs modeled on 2024–2025 cell pricing. The 95% lithium carbonate price spike in two months is the kind of move that breaks project finance assumptions. Developers without fixed-price cell supply agreements or offtake contracts with escalation clauses are exposed.

**U.S. domestic-only LFP manufacturing plays** face a structural cost disadvantage of 2.5–3x versus Chinese turnkey systems that IRA domestic content bonuses partially but not fully offset. Unless tariff escalation or supply chain security premiums paid by hyperscale and defense customers provide a sufficient price umbrella, the economics of standalone U.S. LFP manufacturing remain challenging. The American Battery Factory Arizona project (full capacity in 2028) will be an important data point — but the timeline means the cost competitiveness question won't be answered until the market has potentially shifted further.

**Traditional peaker plant operators** face accelerating displacement risk. With 24 GW of battery storage planned for U.S. commissioning in 2026 and data center customers directly contracting for storage capacity, the use-case that has historically justified gas peaker economics — short-duration, high-margin peak dispatch — is being systematically eroded. This is not new news, but the pace of displacement is now measurably faster than most utility integrated resource plans assumed two years ago.

**Valuation disconnect worth flagging**: The global grid-scale battery storage market is projected at $16.9 billion in 2026 scaling to $90.6 billion by 2033 (27.1% CAGR). At that growth rate, pure-play storage companies with durable market positions are pricing in significant execution risk that may be overstated given the structural demand drivers now in place. The disconnect between actual deployment acceleration and still-cautious public market valuations for storage-exposed equities may represent a window before broader market recognition catches up to the physical infrastructure data.
