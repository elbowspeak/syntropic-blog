## Infrastructure Assessment

**Where things stand as of late May 2026:**

Grid-scale battery storage has crossed from accelerating growth into what can only be described as a deployment surge. The Q1 2026 record of 9.7 GWh in a single quarter — against a full-year 2025 record of 15 GW — means the U.S. is on a pace that, if sustained, would substantially exceed even the aggressive 24 GW annual target. The pipeline is real: interconnection queues, equipment procurement contracts (the LG-Tesla deal alone represents multiple GWh of committed capacity), and utility IRP filings corroborate the headline numbers.

**The principal bottlenecks are no longer technological — they are logistical and geopolitical:**

1. **Transmission interconnection lag**: New storage capacity is being built faster than transmission infrastructure can absorb it. The SunZia HVDC project and the DOE's 7,500-mile transmission expansion plan are necessary but will not deliver relief until the late 2020s. Projects commissioned in 2026 will increasingly face curtailment risk without parallel transmission investment.

2. **Lithium supply chain concentration**: The 70% Chinese refining dependency and the approaching 50% production control figure represent a structural vulnerability that neither Thacker Pass nor Covalent Kwinana fully addresses at current scale. A supply deficit is plausible by 2028 under base-case demand scenarios, which would create a cost floor beneath any optimistic BESS cost trajectory.

3. **Domestic manufacturing ramp timing**: Ford Energy (2027 production start) and LG's Michigan retooling are genuine additions to domestic supply, but they arrive 12–18 months after the peak demand acceleration. The gap between current demand and domestic supply will be filled by imports — primarily from Chinese manufacturers — in the interim.

**Momentum shifts worth noting:**

- Sodium-ion's transition from chemistry research to resolved manufacturing bottlenecks (CATL) is the most significant underlying shift this week. It does not affect 2026 deployments materially, but it changes the 2028–2030 cost and supply chain landscape considerably.
- LDES is moving from pilot projects to capacity stack planning. Form Energy's utility contracts and Eos's IPP formation are the clearest evidence that multi-day storage has achieved planning credibility, even if commercial scale is still limited.
- The VPP policy cluster in Q1 2026 is creating a regulatory foundation that could mobilize gigawatts of behind-the-meter capacity without any new hardware manufacturing — the fastest potential capacity addition on the horizon.

**2. LDES Project Development / IPP Model (Eos Energy / Cerberus — Frontier Power USA)**
Eos's 445% revenue growth combined with the formation of an IPP vehicle represents a business model maturation that the market may not be fully pricing. Pure-play LDES equipment vendors trade on lumpy project revenue; IPP structures generate recurring contracted cash flows that attract infrastructure capital at lower discount rates. Watch for similar structures from Form Energy as its utility contracts mature.

**3. Grid Software and AI-Driven Storage Management (Fluence Energy)**
As the storage fleet scales to tens of gigawatts, the value of intelligent dispatch software increases disproportionately. Fluence's AI-driven energy management platform, combined with its expanding utility contract base across U.S. and European markets, positions it at the intersection of hardware deployment and software margin. This is increasingly a data advantage business: operators with the largest installed bases generate the training data that improves dispatch optimization, creating a compounding competitive position.

**4. HVDC Transmission Infrastructure**
The SunZia project and DOE's 7,500-mile transmission expansion plan, combined with data center load growth potentially exceeding 1,000 TWh globally, create a multi-decade capital deployment cycle in transmission infrastructure. This is less visible than battery headlines but arguably more durable — transmission assets are regulated, long-lived, and not subject to technology obsolescence risk in the same way battery chemistry investments are.

**5. Non-Chinese Lithium Refining (Covalent Lithium — Australia; Rock Tech Lithium — Canada)**
With ~95% of global lithium refining concentrated in China, Argentina, and Chile, and policy pressure building on both sides of the Atlantic to diversify, the scarcity premium on non-Chinese refining capacity is likely to increase. Covalent's Kwinana facility reaching 50,000 MT nameplate capacity by end-2026 and Rock Tech's Ontario converter (up to 36,000 MT/yr, targeting pre-2030 production) are the most advanced Western-aligned projects. Both face execution risk, but the strategic premium commanded by IRA-compliant, non-Chinese supply makes them attractive despite that risk.
