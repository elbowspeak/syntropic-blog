## Infrastructure Assessment

Grid-scale battery storage is in the clearest deployment acceleration in the sector's history. The 36% YoY growth through Q3 2025 is particularly significant because it reflects commissioned, grid-connected capacity — not announcements or pipeline — and it has occurred while LFP cell prices compressed further into the $55–75/kWh range. The cost-deployment feedback loop is working: lower prices drive more projects, more projects drive further manufacturing scale.

**The dominant near-term bottleneck has shifted from technology to interconnection and installation labor.** The DTE / hyperscale data center contract is the clearest evidence of this: a sophisticated industrial customer is using 1.4 GW of storage as an interconnection tool, not purely an energy product. The NYISO queue data (11.9 GW of large load, 8.3 GW added in a single year) confirms that transmission and interconnection queues — not battery availability or cost — are the primary rate-limiting factor in the US.

**Geographic cost gaps remain the most significant structural distortion.** The $230–320/kWh installed cost for 4-hour LFP systems in the US versus $90–130/kWh in China represents a 2.5–3.5x premium that cannot be explained by labor costs alone. Tariffs, supply chain immaturity, and permitting friction account for the remainder. This gap is the central policy problem facing US energy storage buildout, and the DOE loan commitments ($2.3B for Thacker Pass, $996M for Rhyolite Ridge, and others) represent the federal government's bet that domestic supply chain investment can close it within the decade.

**Long-duration storage is transitioning from demonstration to early commercial phase.** Form Energy's Minnesota operational data, the 500 MW sodium-ion project in China, and the 2,400 MWh Chinese CAES facility all represent LDES assets that are generating real operational data — a qualitative shift from 2023–2024. LDES deployment is still measured in GWh rather than tens of GWh, but the 49% growth in 2025 (to 15 GWh cumulative) suggests the category is beginning its own S-curve.

**Solid-state batteries remain in the pre-commercial phase** outside of limited semi-solid deployments in China. The 2027 small-scale production timelines from BYD and GBT are credible directionally but have consistently slipped. The honest assessment is that solid-state will not affect grid-scale storage economics in this analysis window.

**1. US Interconnection Infrastructure & Grid Services**
The NYISO data (11.9 GW queued, 8.3 GW added in 2025 alone) and the DTE / hyperscale data center contract point to a persistent, multi-year shortage of interconnection capacity and grid services in load-dense markets. Companies providing interconnection study services, transmission upgrades, and grid-scale storage specifically positioned for load interconnection support are in a structurally advantaged position. **2. Sodium-Ion Battery Supply Chain (Selectively)**
Peak Energy's US commercial agreement with Jupiter Power is a marker worth tracking. If sodium-ion reaches $40/kWh by 2027, the companies building supply chain infrastructure for sodium precursor materials (primarily sodium carbonate, iron, and manganese — all widely available domestically) are positioned for a supply chain that is far less geopolitically constrained than lithium. The risk is execution: CATL and BYD's manufacturing ramp timelines have historically been optimistic.

**3. Long-Duration Storage Project Development**
The LDES market reaching $4.84B in 2024 and growing toward $13B by 2032, combined with DOE's $100M in non-lithium storage pilot funding, creates a funded pipeline for early-stage LDES project developers. Form Energy's operational milestone is the most significant near-term catalyst. Infrastructure funds with a 10–15 year hold horizon should be actively underwriting LDES project equity alongside traditional lithium-ion storage.

**4. US Lithium Mining & Processing**
The 86% YTD lithium price recovery and DOE loan commitments for Thacker Pass ($2.3B), Rhyolite Ridge ($996M), and Project ATLiS ($1.4B conditional) represent a significant improvement in project economics for assets that were deeply uneconomic at 2024 lithium prices. The VAT rebate change in China simultaneously reduces competitive pressure from Chinese battery imports, improving the long-term revenue outlook for US battery manufacturing that these assets would supply.

**5. Underground Transmission Development**
The Champlain Hudson Power Express's design (300+ miles entirely underground/underwater) is expensive but permitting-viable in a way that above-ground lines are not in densely populated corridors. As the Northeast faces growing load from data centers and building electrification, the underground HVDC template established by CHPE will be referenced repeatedly. Engineering firms, cable manufacturers, and project developers with underground HVDC expertise are in a supply-constrained specialty.

The cobalt supply constraint from DRC quotas reinforces this — higher cobalt prices increase the relative cost disadvantage of cobalt-containing chemistries for stationary applications where energy density is not the primary constraint.

**2. Western Battery Manufacturers Without Clear Cost Roadmaps**

Without a credible path to within 30–40% of Chinese cost structures, Western manufacturers are competing primarily on supply chain security and political preference — valuable but fragile moats that depend on policy continuity.

**3. Projects Without Secured Interconnection**
The interconnection queue crisis is becoming a project-killer, not merely a delay factor. Developers holding battery storage projects without secured interconnection agreements in congested ISOs (NYISO, PJM, CAISO) face years of queue uncertainty that undermines return profiles. The premium on projects with secured or near-secured interconnection is likely underpriced by the market relative to the actual optionality value.

**4. Solid-State Battery Pure-Plays (Near-Term)**
With BYD targeting 2027 for small-scale production and GBT targeting GWh-scale by 2026 (a target that warrants skepticism), the commercialization timeline for solid-state continues to extend. Companies or funds with near-term return requirements predicated on solid-state battery commercialization before 2028 should reassess timeline assumptions. The technology will matter — but likely not within a 24-month investment horizon.
