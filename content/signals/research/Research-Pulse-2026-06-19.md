## Weekly Digest

This week's most significant finds center on Michael Levin's expanding theoretical framework: two-part "Mind Everywhere" paper with Resnik systematically addresses what makes any system—biological, engineered, or hybrid—genuinely goal-directed and teleological, while the Rouleau & Levin piece on consciousness maps theories of mind onto unconventional (pre-neural) embodiments, directly bridging bioelectricity and philosophy of mind. On the complexity side, Heylighen offers a clarifying conceptual treatment of emergence and self-organization arguing these phenomena are simpler and more natural than typically assumed, and a stochastic model demonstrates that higher-order collective behavior can emerge purely from shared environments without direct inter-agent interaction—a result with implications for evolutionary and developmental thinking. The self-organization criticality paper (EvoSK) exploring ergodicity breaking in adaptive systems is also notable for its relevance to dissipative structure theory and adaptive evolution.
